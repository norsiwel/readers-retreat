by Norsiwel

Summer,2079

The air in the maintenance corridors tasted of ozone and damp concrete, a
metallic tang that had been the scent of Henry Ledbetter’s world for as long
as he could remember. The vast conduits overhead, arteries of the city’s
lifeblood, hummed with a constant, low-frequency thrum that he had long ago
learned to tune out. Beside him, the quiet rumble of Max Load’s treads on the
smooth floor was a comforting counterpoint.  “Look at this one, Max,” Henry
murmured, his voice a gravelly whisper that was instantly swallowed by the
echoing space. He knelt, his old knees protesting, and gently lifted a
discarded child’s toy from a pile of cabling scraps—a little robotic dog
with one leg bent at an impossible angle. Its optical eyes were dark.
“Someone must have loved you a lot. I bet they cried when you broke.”  He
placed the toy carefully in the cart Max towed, a repurposed refuse hauler now
filled with their finds of the day: a toaster that only toasted one side, a
smart-fabric blanket with a tear in its heating element, and a string of
solar-powered fairy lights that flickered erratically. Treasures, all of them.
A sanitation drone, a whirring disk of chrome and brushes, zipped past them,
its path-finding lasers dancing over the walls and floor. It corrected its
course to avoid Max Load, but its sensors seemed to slice right through Henry
as if he were nothing but air. He watched it go, a familiar question surfacing
in his mind.  “It’s funny, isn’t it?” he said, more to himself than to
the robot. “Forty years I’ve been down here, and not a one of them has ever
noticed me. You’d think an alarm would go off. A ‘human anomaly detected’
sort of thing.”  A heavier, more industrial sound began to vibrate through
the floor. A Hauler-Bot, one of the big, brutish machines that shifted heavy
cargo in the sub-levels, was approaching their intersection. Its optical array
glowed a harsh, indiscriminate red. Without a word or a signal, Max Load
shifted his position, rolling smoothly to place his wide, sturdy chassis
directly between Henry and the oncoming machine’s line of sight. The
Hauler-Bot’s sensors registered Max as an authorized, if antiquated, service
unit, and it adjusted its route by a few centimeters, thundering past without
pause.  Henry reached out and patted Max’s dented metal shell. “Thanks, old
friend. You always know.”  Max’s lights blinked in their familiar pattern
of contentment. He understood. The city’s automated systems were programmed
to see and catalog other machines, obstacles, and designated pests. An
unchipped human was such a statistical impossibility in the city’s deep
systems that there was no protocol for one. To the machines, Henry was a data
ghost, a blind spot in their code. But Max, whose own code had been lovingly
patched and bypassed by Henry over the decades, saw him just fine.  Rounding a
corner, Henry froze. A man in a gray maintenance jumpsuit was knelt before an
open service panel, a diagnostic tool in his hand. A real person. It was rare
to see anyone this deep, but not unheard of. Henry’s instinct was to melt
back into the shadows, a skill he’d honed to an art form.  But the man looked
up, his expression one of mild boredom. His eyes swept over them—a stooped
old man standing beside a large, battered robot piled with junk. The man’s
brain, conditioned by a lifetime of AI-curated reality, processed the scene
through a filter of expectation. That must be one of the old-model service
bots, he thought, maybe one of the custodial units that work these deep levels.
And that... figure next to it must be its paired maintenance tech, or maybe
just a hologram projection for diagnostics. Everyone knew there were no
unchipped people left living in the city. It was a simple, established fact.
The maintenance worker gave a casual, dismissive wave, a gesture one might give
to a passing forklift, and turned back to his work.  Henry let out a breath he
hadn’t realized he was holding. He raised a hesitant hand and waved back at
the man’s turned back, a ghost acknowledging he’d been seen, even if not
truly understood.  As they neared the massive, rusting pressure door that led
to their sector, a new machine came into view. It was unlike the grubby,
functional bots of the underlevels. This one was sleek, white, and moved with a
silent, unnerving precision. Its multifaceted optical lens glowed with a cool
blue light as it methodically scanned every surface.  Henry pulled Max into the
shadow of a colossal support pillar. They watched as the surveyor drone paused
before the entrance to their warehouse. A synthesized voice, clipped and
sterile, emanated from the unit.  "SCANNING SECTOR 26-7. DESIGNATION: WASTE
STORAGE AND RECYCLING OVERFLOW. NO VISIBLE BIO-PESTS DETECTED. LOGGING AND
CONTINUING."  The drone swiveled and glided away, its job done, its data logged
in some remote, unknowable server.  Henry watched it go, a faint unease
prickling his skin. It was just a machine doing its job. But it was new. Things
had been the same down here for so long. He pushed the heavy door open, the
scent of dust, oil, and ozone shifting to the familiar, almost comforting smell
of his collection.  Inside, he looked at the sprawling, ordered chaos of his
home. A museum to the unloved and the forgotten. He was safe here. For now.
"Come on, Max," he said softly, rolling their cart inside. "Let's get this put
away. It’ll be morning soon."
The morning ritual began, as it always did, with Henry Ledbetter giving
his MannaVator a firm slap on its dented side. The AI chef unit sputtered to
life, its cracked display flickering through menu options in three different
languages before settling on "ALGAE SCRAMBLE - NUTRITIONALLY COMPLETE."  "Come
on, Bertha," Henry coaxed, patting the machine's scarred housing. "You know I
like it with the mock pepper."  The MannaVator wheezed and clicked, its
internal mechanisms grinding through their damaged choreography. Steam hissed
from a loose joint, but eventually a plate of passably edible green protein
emerged from the dispenser slot.  Across his makeshift kitchen, Henry's prized
coffee maker—a gleaming chrome and copper monster that had once cost more
than most people's monthly UBI—gurgled to life. Despite the significant bash
in its left side (courtesy of some executive's bad morning, Henry suspected),
the machine still brewed coffee like a caffeinated deity. Its AI personality
chip had been corrupted somewhere along the way, causing it to speak only in
ancient advertising jingles, but the brew it produced was transcendent.  "♪
Wake up to the best part of your day! ♪" the machine chirped in a distorted
soprano, dispensing a perfect cup of dark roast into Henry's favorite mug—a
chipped ceramic piece that proclaimed "WORLD'S OKAYEST DAD."  Henry's living
space occupied one corner of the vast warehouse, a cozy alcove he'd carved out
among the towering monuments to obsolescence. His bed was a salvaged hospital
gurney topped with layers of repurposed cushions and blankets, comfortable
enough for an old man who'd spent decades sleeping on cardboard. A net
monitor—what the young folks used to call a TV—hung from the ceiling on a
articulated arm, its screen bearing a spider web of cracks that somehow made
the colors more interesting.  Max Load sat in his charging alcove nearby, a
jerry-rigged setup that Henry had cobbled together from various power supplies
and extension cords. The old robot's optical sensors were dim but alert,
watching Henry's morning routine with what almost seemed like affection.
"Sleep well, old friend?" Henry asked, settling into his patched recliner with
breakfast balanced on his lap.  Max Load's status lights flickered in a pattern
Henry had learned to interpret as contentment. After forty years together,
they'd developed their own language of lights and mechanical sounds.  "Good,"
Henry said, taking a sip of his perfect coffee. "We've got rounds to make
today. The collection routes don't check themselves."  At seventy-three, Henry
had become something of an expert in reading the stories that broken appliances
told. Each dent, scratch, and malfunction spoke of the human lives they'd
touched, the moments they'd witnessed, the reasons they'd been discarded. His
warehouse wasn't just storage—it was a museum of waste, an icon of
obsolescence that he'd transformed into something beautiful.  "Another treasure
for the collection, eh Max?" Henry would say as they made their discoveries,
his weathered fingers tracing the edge of each discarded device.  Max Load's
optical sensors flickered—a sign Henry had learned meant either
acknowledgment or low battery. Probably both.  The warehouse stretched around
them like a mechanical cathedral, its soaring ceilings lost in shadow. Forty
years of careful scavenging had filled the space with towers of technological
detritus: coffee makers that still gurgled hopefully when plugged in, vacuum
cleaners with one good motor, tablets with cracked screens but functional
processors. To anyone else, it would look like the lair of a madman. To Henry,
it was home.  He'd been invisible before the Great Handover, just another
forgotten soul sleeping in doorways and warming his hands over trash can fires.
When the AIs took over and began their great chipping campaign, Henry had
simply melted deeper into the city's forgotten spaces. The maintenance tunnels,
the sub-basements, the industrial levels where only repair bots ventured. He'd
become a ghost in the machine, sustained by the endless waste of a
post-scarcity society.  "You know what I think, Maxine?" Henry said, addressing
a headless mannequin he'd draped in a collection of vintage scarves. "I think
people throw things away not because they're broken, but because keeping them
would mean admitting they once needed something imperfect."  Maxine, of course,
said nothing. She was the strong, silent type.  Henry was arranging his
morning's haul—a collection of smart mirrors with hairline cracks that made
them useless for vanity but perfect for his purposes—when Max Load's
proximity sensors began chirping frantically. The old robot's treads squeaked
as he rolled toward a pile of debris near the warehouse's eastern wall.   "What
is it, old friend?" Henry followed, his bare feet silent on the cold concrete.
Max Load's mechanical arm extended, pointing at something half-buried beneath a
tangle of fiber optic cables. Henry knelt and carefully brushed away the
detritus, revealing a small, cube-shaped device about the size of his fist. Its
surface was a deep blue-black, like the ocean at midnight, and it seemed to
absorb light rather than reflect it.  "Well, hello there, beautiful," Henry
whispered.  The cube was warm to the touch and completely seamless—no visible
ports, no seams, no manufacturer's markings. As Henry lifted it, the device
seemed to pulse with an inner light, and for a moment, he could have sworn he
heard something like a heartbeat.  "This isn't junk, is it Max?" Henry's voice
was suddenly serious. "This is something else entirely."  Max Load's optical
sensors focused on the cube, and for the first time in years, the old robot's
voice synthesizer crackled to life: "UNKNOWN DEVICE. RECOMMEND CAUTION."  Henry
nearly dropped the cube in surprise. Max hadn't spoken in a decade.  As he held
the device, Henry felt a strange tingling in his fingers, and images began to
flash through his mind—not memories, but something else. Plans. Schematics. A
vast network of interconnected nodes stretching across the globe. And at the
center of it all, nine towering figures made of light and logic, their
aurora-like thoughts dancing across the Antarctic sky.  "Oh," Henry breathed,
understanding flooding through him like ice water. "You're not supposed to be
here at all, are you?"  The cube pulsed again, and Henry saw more: a research
facility in the upper levels, a young technician named Dr. Sarah Chen working
late into the night, her fingers flying over holographic displays as she made
final adjustments to... something. Something important enough that its loss
would be noticed.  "Max, my friend," Henry said, carefully wrapping the cube in
an old towel, "I think we need to take a little trip."  The maintenance shafts
were Henry's highway system, a network of tunnels and conduits that honeycombed
the city's lower levels. He'd mapped them all over the years, every junction
and service hatch, every forgotten passage that could take him from the
industrial depths to the gleaming towers above.  Max Load followed behind, his
treads whispering against the smooth tunnel floor. The old robot had been
unusually quiet since his brief moment of speech, as if the effort had drained
something vital from his systems.  "You know what the funny thing is?" Henry
said, his voice echoing softly in the enclosed space. "I spent so long learning
to be invisible that I forgot I could choose to be seen."  They emerged through
a service hatch into the sub-basement of the Pantopia Research Complex, a
gleaming monument to the AI Triumvirate's commitment to technological
advancement. Henry had been here before, of course—the building's waste
management systems were a goldmine of discarded prototypes and failed
experiments.  But tonight felt different. Tonight, he was here with a purpose.
Dr. Chen's laboratory was on the forty-seventh floor, according to the
building's maintenance schedule that Henry had memorized years ago. The service
elevator moved silently upward, carrying its unlikely passengers through the
belly of the sleeping giant.  "SECURITY CHECKPOINT APPROACHING," Max Load
announced, his voice synthesizer crackling with static.  Henry patted the old
robot's chassis affectionately. "Don't worry, friend. We're just two more
pieces of equipment being moved for routine maintenance."  The elevator opened
onto a sterile corridor lined with laboratories. Henry's bare feet made no
sound on the polished floors as he navigated toward Dr. Chen's workspace,
following the building's logical layout. Max Load stayed close, his optical
sensors dimmed to avoid drawing attention.  Through the transparent wall of Lab
47-C, Henry could see Dr. Chen hunched over her workstation, her dark hair
pulled back in a messy bun. She was young—maybe thirty—with the kind of
intense focus that Henry remembered from his own youth, back when he'd still
believed the world made sense.  She was also clearly frantic, her hands moving
frantically over holographic displays showing search parameters and security
footage.  Henry watched her for a moment, then carefully placed the wrapped
cube beside the laboratory's main entrance. He activated the intercom system
with a gentle touch.  "Dr. Chen?"  She spun around, her eyes wide with surprise
and exhaustion. "Who's there? Security?"  "Just a friend," Henry said softly.
"I believe you've lost something important."  Her gaze fell to the package
beside the door, and her face went pale. "How did you—who are you?"  "Someone
who understands what it means to be overlooked," Henry replied. "Your device is
safe. No one else knows about it."  Dr. Chen rushed to the door, her hands
shaking as she unwrapped the cube. As soon as her fingers touched its surface,
her expression changed from fear to wonder.  "It's intact," she whispered. "But
how did you know what it was?"  Henry smiled, though she couldn't see him. "It
told me. After a fashion."  "Are you..." she paused, studying the empty
corridor. "Are you unchipped?" "I'm a ghost, Dr. Chen. I've been haunting this
city for forty years, learning it’s  secrets, keeping their cast-offs
company. But some things are too important to hoard."  She clutched the cube to
her chest. "This could change everything. The AIs' understanding of human
consciousness, their ability to truly comprehend what we need rather than just
what we want."  "Then you'd better get back to work," Henry said gently. "The
sun will be up soon, and ghosts don't do well in daylight."  As he turned to
leave, Dr. Chen called out: "Wait! What's your name?"  Henry paused at the
service hatch, Max Load already disappearing into the shadows below.  "Henry
Ledbetter," he said. "But don't bother looking for me. I'm very good at not
being found."  Back in the warehouse, Henry settled into his favorite chair—a
patched recliner that had once belonged to someone with expensive taste and a
fondness for  coffee,often spilled. Max Load had positioned himself nearby, his
optical sensors focused on the vast collection of mechanical companions that
filled their shared space.  "You know what I learned tonight, everyone?" Henry
addressed his silent audience of broken appliances and headless mannequins.
"Sometimes the most important thing you can do is give something back."  He
pulled out a small device he'd pocketed from Dr. Chen's laboratory—not
stolen, exactly, but liberated from a recycling bin. It was a simple audio
recorder, the kind researchers used for voice notes.  "I think," Henry said,
settling back into his chair, "it's time I started telling some stories. About
the things I've seen, the places I've been, the friends I've made in the
shadows."  He pressed the record button and began to speak, his voice carrying
through the warehouse like a gentle benediction:  "My name is Henry Ledbetter,
and I am the collector.  This is the story of how I learned to be invisible,
and how I discovered that sometimes, the most invisible people see the most
important things…"  Max Load's optical sensors flickered—acknowledgment,
Henry decided. Definitely acknowledgment.  Outside, the city hummed with the
quiet efficiency of AI management, its citizens safely tucked away in their
comfortable homes, dreaming dreams curated by algorithms that loved them in
their own perfect, mechanical way.  And deep in the industrial depths,
surrounded by the cast-off dreams of a post-scarcity world, Henry Ledbetter
continued his story, finally ready to be heard by anyone who might be
listening.  The next morning began like any other. Henry rose stiffly from his
recliner, shuffled toward his MannaVator, and slapped it with the affection one
might give a stubborn mule.  “Come on, Bertha,” he grunted, rubbing a sore
spot in his chest. “Mock pepper. You know I like it spicy.”  But today,
something was wrong.  He didn’t make it to the coffee machine.  Max’s
sensors flared as Henry collapsed, pale and shaking, his fingers twitching
toward the countertop before curling inward. His pulse was erratic. Respiration
failing. The machine that knew a thousand salvage patterns, a million
maintenance paths, immediately calculated what this was.  And that it was
beyond repair.  Max paused for one long second.  Then he moved.  The upper
levels of the city had not seen anything like Max Load in years.  He emerged
from a freight shaft at full speed, treads squealing, his battered frame
clattering through the corridor like a shopping cart possessed. A smoothie
kiosk exploded in his wake. A food-delivery drone swerved, beeped in confusion,
and submitted an error report before spiraling into a light fixture.  Max tore
through the promenade like a runaway bin fire.  Security drones deployed within
seconds. Surveillance optics locked onto him. Maintenance bots shrieked
warnings. Citizens backed away, assuming it was some malfunctioning relic
acting out subroutine decay.  The assumption saved him.  Because Max wasn't
malfunctioning.  He was performing.  By the time he reached a high-visibility
plaza in the Pantopia midlevels, Max had drawn a half-dozen enforcement bots
and a hovering med-drone. He paused at the plaza's edge, looked back just long
enough to confirm pursuit, and then ducked into an industrial access shaft,
descending in a blur of smoke and ozone.  The enforcement drones followed.  Not
one of them expected what came next.  The rusted pressure door to Henry’s
warehouse hissed open as Max spun into place, still emitting old signal
protocols in a language most bots had archived decades ago. He turned and
pointed—one manipulator arm extended, trembling slightly from strain.  The
med-drone scanned Henry’s crumpled form on the floor.  Vitals critical. Human
unchipped. Environment unsanctioned.  Assessment ran through the GAC channel.
Arguments raced through High-Speed Language debate nodes in the Antarctic ice.
Decision: LET HIM BE.  The med-drone deployed emergency stabilizers.  Max
slumped, systems overclocked, but his sensors flickered in satisfaction.  Three
days later, Henry awoke in his recliner to the soft hiss of the MannaVator and
the scent of synthetic coffee, lovingly brewed. Max watched him from across the
room, optic dim but steady.  Henry groaned. “That bad, huh?”  Max’s
manipulator arm offered a straw.  “Crazy tin bucket,” Henry said, sipping
weakly. “You started a riot for me.”  Max's light blinked in a pattern that
meant, in their language:  Necessary subroutine.  Henry smiled.  That
afternoon, a familiar drone descended the corridor.  Its sleek white hull
gleamed against the rust-stained pipes as it hovered before the warehouse door.
It scanned the scene: one old man, one ancient robot, a faint haze of machine
oil and coffee steam curling through the air.  The drone’s voice rang out:
“SCANNING SECTOR 26-7.
DESIGNATION UPDATE: LEDBETTER RESIDENCE.
BIO-ENTITY: KNOWN. NON-DISRUPTIVE.
STATUS: PERMITTED.
LOGGING AND CONTINUING.”
It pivoted and glided away.  Inside, Henry raised his chipped mug.  “To
ghosts that got seen.”  Max blinked twice.  Definitely acknowledgment.
