                                     There Are None So Blind
                                            by Norsiwel

Bronze gears turned with impossible precision, each tooth filed to mirror the heavens. The Helios Dial could predict eclipses, tides, even the rising of storms weeks in advance, a machine centuries ahead of its age. Callista of Rhodes bent over it in her workshop, oil lamp gleaming on the polished bronze, when the chants of war from the Agora below rattled her shutters. The Gods had nothing to do with it.

The Athenian air in 415 BCE hummed with a dangerous energy, thick with the scent of salt, olive oil, and the fervent rhetoric of impending war. In the Agora below, citizens clustered around speakers who promised swift victory over Sicily, their voices rising in fervent agreement. "Alcibiades will lead us to glory!" they chanted. "The gods favor Athens!" 

From her workshop window, Callista of Rhodes watched the crowd with growing unease, then returned to her work table where bronze gleamed under oil lamplight.  Before her lay the Helios Dial  a marvel of bronze cogs meshing in precise ratios, its central gear wheel marked with the nineteen-year Metonic cycle that reconciled solar and lunar time, smaller wheels tracking the wandering stars through their epicycles. 

Differential gears, inspired by the water-lifting mechanisms of Alexandria, translated the complex dance of celestial periods into a single, readable display. It predicted the Nile's floods, the moon's eclipse, the safe passage across the wine-dark sea. Not magic, but meticulous observation forged into mechanism.  The device drew upon principles she'd learned from Syracusan mechanics  the gear trains that lifted massive stones for their temples, the astronomical calculations inherited from Babylonian star-watchers, all wedded to the precision gear-cutting techniques that made their finest water clocks possible. 

Each bronze tooth was hand-filed to mathematical perfection, each gear ratio calculated to mirror the cosmic periodicities that governed earthly phenomena. The parapegma markings around its rim correlated star positions with weather patterns, while the astrolabe projections mapped the celestial sphere onto its rotating face.  Trained in the Ionian school, Callista saw the world as a complex, knowable machine. Her water clocks measured time with impartial precision, her surgical tools saved lives based on anatomical truths, not prayers. Yet, this very empiricism made her an outsider.

 Traditionalists whispered she insulted the gods by seeking natural causes for divine phenomena. Her brilliance was tolerated, barely, for its useful creations. The Helios Dial, however, was different. It held too much power.  "Teacher?" A young voice broke her concentration. Leandros, her earnest protégé, stood in the doorway, his face flushed with excitement. "Have you heard? The Assembly voted!

 Alcibiades will lead the greatest fleet ever assembled! Surely this is the gods' will  and your Dial will guide us to certain victory!"  Callista set down her stylus carefully. "Leandros, victory is never certain. The Dial shows patterns, probabilities. It warns of storms, not of glory."  "But the people say—"  "The people," Callista interrupted gently, "want to believe in miracles. 

They want to believe their leaders are touched by divine favor. It's easier than accepting that we're all merely human, fallible and mortal."  Before Leandros could respond, the sound of marching feet echoed from the street. Alcibiades himself appeared in the doorway moments later, a whirlwind of polished armor and intoxicating confidence. His eyes, sharp as obsidian, locked onto the Helios Dial, gleaming under the oil lamp.
  
"A gift from Athena herself!" Alcibiades declared, his voice resonating in the confined space. He didn't ask; he claimed. "This divine instrument, Callista, will guide our glorious Sicilian Expedition to swift victory. Imagine! Unfailing navigation, knowledge of storms before they strike, proof of the gods' favor shining upon our fleet!" 
 
Callista met his gaze, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. "General, the Helios Dial is no talisman. Its predictions are based on celestial mechanics, complex calculations. The gear ratios must be precisely calibrated to the seasons, the differential wheels properly aligned to account for the moon's varying speed through its orbit.
 
Misinterpreted, misused, it could lead to disaster as easily as triumph. It requires understanding, not blind faith."  Alcibiades laughed, a sound like bronze scraping stone. "Understanding? The people understand spectacle! They understand proof of divine mandate! Look at them in the streets  they hunger for a sign that Athens is blessed, that their sacrifices matter." 

His voice dropped, became calculating. "The Assembly has already voted, Callista. The Dial serves Athens now." He gestured, and soldiers moved towards the device.  Leandros stepped forward, his earlier excitement now tempered with uncertainty. "Teacher, perhaps... perhaps if it gives the people hope? If it helps them believe in the cause?" 
 
Callista looked from her creation to her student, seeing the same dangerous hunger in his eyes that filled the streets below. "Hope built on lies, Leandros," she said, her voice low and fierce, "crumbles at the first touch of reality. And when it falls, it buries those who trusted it."  But the soldiers lifted the Dial, carrying away her life's work. 

The choice was made for her: comply or face immediate ruin.  In the chaotic din of the Piraeus shipyards, the Helios Dial became a beacon of false certainty. Alcibiades paraded it before the masses and the assembled triremes. Priests anointed it; orators proclaimed it the literal hand of Athena guiding Athenian destiny. 

"Unfailable navigation!" became the rallying cry, fueling the already dangerous overconfidence of the expedition.  Damon, a grizzled ship captain, approached Callista as she stood watching the ceremony. "Begging your pardon, lady, but that contraption of yours  it really tells the future?"  "It predicts weather patterns, Captain. Tides, winds, seasonal changes. Nothing more, nothing less."  

Damon's weathered face creased with concern. "And the General? He understands this?"  Callista met his eyes. "What do you think, Captain?"  The old sailor spat into the harbor. "I think we're sailing into a storm of our own making. But the boys..." He gestured toward the young sailors loading supplies, their faces bright with anticipation.
 
"They believe. They think the gods themselves are steering our ships."  "And you, Captain? What do you believe?"  "I believe in reading the sky, checking my supplies, and keeping my men alive. Not in golden boys who've never felt salt spray freeze on their faces." He paused, studying her. "You'll be aboard the flagship?"  "As technical advisor. A prisoner dressed as an honor."  

Damon nodded grimly. "Then perhaps someone with sense will survive this madness."  Forced onto Alcibiades' flagship, Callista became a prisoner of war wrapped in the guise of honor. Her keen mind saw the fatal flaws in the planning, the underestimation of Sicily, the sheer hubris. 
One moonless night, as the fleet moved through darkened waters, a figure slipped into her cramped quarters  a man with dark, observant eyes and features marking him as Nubian. He spoke softly, urgently.  "Callista of Rhodes. I am Khenti, a scholar from Meroe. We watch. We guard knowledge."

 His gaze flickered towards where the Dial was kept under heavy guard. "Your device... it is brilliance. But brilliance in the hands of the reckless is a spark in dry tinder."  "You speak of the Dial?"  "I speak of more than bronze and gears." Khenti's voice dropped to barely a whisper. "I have seen things, heard conversations not meant for common ears. Your golden general…"

He paused, seeming to weigh his words. "He is not what the people believe him to be."  "What do you mean?"  Khenti glanced toward the door, then leaned closer. "Three nights ago, I served wine at a private gathering of the expedition's commanders. Alcibiades was deep in his cups, speaking freely to his inner circle. He called his own soldiers 'useful fools' and 'expendable sheep.' 

He spoke of how the people 'deserve whatever happens to them' for being so easily manipulated."  Callista felt ice in her veins. "You're certain?"  "I heard him say, 'Let them die gloriously for Athens  the people love a tragic hero more than a living coward. When we return, defeated but defiant, they'll worship us even more.'" Khenti's eyes burned with quiet fury. "He plans for failure, Callista. He sees death as a tool for building his legend."  
"But the expedition"  

"Is a means to an end. Win, and he's a conquering hero. Lose magnificently, and he's a martyred god. Either way, Alcibiades wins." Khenti stood, preparing to leave. "Your device could shift balances, empower tyrants, drown the Mediterranean in blood if its secrets are plundered. But the greater danger may be the man who wields it. Is knowledge worth such a price?" 
 
His words struck deep. Was her pursuit of truth inherently noble, regardless of consequence? And what was her responsibility when she now knew the truth about the man leading thousands to their deaths?  As Sicily neared, the sky bruised with the approach of the storm the Dial had predicted days earlier. Mountains of water crashed against the triremes. 

Panicked captains crowded around the Dial, its complex gears now symbols of terror rather than hope. They misread its warnings, argued over its symbols. Alcibiades, his face a mask of fury, demanded it show him a safe path.  "Turn northwest!" one captain bellowed, pointing at a gear cluster. "The Dial points to the Bear!"  "Fool!" another countered. "That alignment is for the season, not the storm! We must ride it out south!"  Captain Damon, his voice cutting through the chaos, shouted, "Belay that! Where's the woman who built this thing?" 

His eyes found Callista. "Lady! If you've got wisdom, now's the time!"  The ships scattered, some turning towards the shouted interpretation  directly towards a jagged coastline revealed only by lightning flashes. Callista could bear it no longer. Shoving past guards, she lunged for the Dial, ignoring Alcibiades' roar. 

Her fingers flew over the gears, aligning the solar pointer with the season's mark, cranking the lunar calculator to account for the month's phase. The differential wheels spun as she calculated the storm's true path  the intersecting circles of celestial influence mapped onto earthly phenomena, the same principles that predicted the Nile's floods now revealing wind patterns through the marriage of star positions and seasonal pressure. 

"Hard to starboard! NOW!" she screamed at the helmsman. "Follow that bearing!" A handful of ships, including Damon's, reacted swiftly to her undeniable authority in that moment, their captains trusting seamanship over spectacle. They lurched away from the deadly rocks.  The others, including Alcibiades' own ship momentarily delayed by his indecision, were not so lucky. 

The sickening crunch of wood on stone echoed over the wind as several ships were driven onto the rocks. Young sailors  boys who had believed in divine protection  screamed as they were swallowed by the dark waters.  Alcibiades, drenched and livid, turned on Callista as his ship narrowly avoided the fate of others. "Treason!" he spat, water streaming down his face. "You sabotaged the Dial! You conspire with the storm gods against Athens! Seize her!" 
 
As soldiers dragged her below, Callista caught sight of Leandros, his face pale with the horror of what he'd witnessed. The blind faith in his eyes had been replaced by something darker, more questioning.  That night, tormented by guilt over the lost ships and the weight of Khenti's revelation, she found her student waiting in the shadows of the brig.

Without a word, he slipped a file between the bars, his hands shaking.  "Teacher," he whispered, "I heard what he said about the storm. About the men who died. He called them 'acceptable losses' for the greater glory of Athens." His voice cracked. "But they weren't acceptable to their mothers, were they?"  Callista's escape into the storm-lashed Sicilian night was a desperate blur, but she carried with her more than just the memory of bronze gears and celestial calculations. 

She carried the truth about the man who would lead Athens to ruin.  The Sicilian Expedition became a charnel house. Besieging Syracuse, plagued by disease, divided leadership, and fierce local resistance, the Athenian forces disintegrated. Callista, surviving on her wits and medical skills among the scattered remnants, witnessed the final, brutal collapse.
 
She saw Alcibiades abandon his men when defeat became inevitable, fleeing to save his own skin while thousands of young Athenians died in the stone quarries of Syracuse.  In the chaotic retreat, a fire engulfed the command tent. Through the smoke, she saw the gleam of the Helios Dial melting, its precision-cut gears warping, its celestial map consumed by flame  reason devoured by hubris, science perverted by vanity. 

Months later, Callista returned to Athens, not in triumph, but in the ragged clothes of a refugee. The city that had launched a thousand ships with divine certainty was a shadow of itself. Famine stalked the streets. The dreaded Plague had returned. 

Demagogues, louder and more vicious than before, shrieked from the Pnyx. Alcibiades had fled, accused of worse crimes, but the people's hunger for strongmen remained undiminished.  Cleon, a voice of crude, violent populism, now held sway over the Assembly. He summoned Callista to face the gathered citizens. The air reeked of despair and unwashed bodies. Cleon pointed a thick finger at her.  "Behold! The creator of Athena's betrayed gift! 

The gods punish us for her failure! But redemption is possible!" His voice boomed over the restless crowd. "Rebuild the Dial, Callista! Restore the sacred instrument! Let divine favor shine once more upon Athens! Do this, and your past... errors... may be forgiven." 
 
The crowd roared approval, their hollow eyes bright with desperate hope. They wanted another miracle, another golden savior to worship. Callista looked out at them  the same people who had cheered Alcibiades, who had sent their sons to die for his glory, who were now ready to repeat the cycle with Cleon.  The easy path was clear: rebuild, survive, give them what they wanted. 
But the weight of the broken Dial, the screams in the Sicilian storm, the faces of the dead, pressed upon her. 

Most of all, she remembered Khenti's words and the terrible truth about the man they had deified.  She stepped forward, not towards Cleon, but towards the center of the Assembly floor. Her voice, though weary, cut through the murmurs with the precision of a scalpel.
  
"Citizens of Athens," she began, her gaze sweeping over the crowd, "you seek a god's power without a god's wisdom. You demanded a miracle and called it science. You took a man  flawed, selfish, mortal  and made him a god in your minds. And when he failed you, as mortals always do, you called it betrayal rather than recognizing your own folly."  

Angry murmurs rippled through the crowd, but she pressed on. "My machine was no oracle handed down from Olympus. It was a mirror. And in its polished bronze, you saw only your own boundless hunger for easy answers reflected back. You saw conquest, glory, divine right. You never saw the responsibility that knowledge demands, the humility that wisdom requires." 
 
She held up not tools, but a bundle of scrolls. "Here is the truth of the Helios Dial. Here are the gear ratios, the celestial calculations, the principles of epicycles and differential mechanisms. Not as a weapon for demagogues, but as knowledge for those who seek understanding without the need for worship."  Her voice rose, carrying across the Assembly. 

"Stop looking for gods among men! Stop surrendering your judgment to golden voices and silver tongues! The leader who serves you best is not the one who promises miracles, but the one who admits their limitations. Not the one who claims divine favor, but the one who remembers they are your servant, not your master."  

She turned, not to Cleon, but towards the distant horizon. "I consign these scrolls to the scholars of the future, wherever they may gather wisdom  perhaps in that fabled Library they whisper of in Alexandria. Let knowledge grow in soil watered by humility, not blood spilled for vanity."  The silence that followed was deafening. 

Then Cleon's face purpled with rage. 

"Exile!" he bellowed. "Exile for this traitor to Athens and the gods!"  But in the crowd, she saw some faces that looked thoughtful rather than angry. Leandros stood among them, no longer the eager boy who had sought easy answers, but a young man learning to ask difficult questions.  The shores of a small, rocky island in the Aegean welcomed no one but fishermen and goats. Here, Callista of Rhodes found a harsh peace. 

Using salvaged tools, she built a simple shelter. The sound of the sea replaced the roar of the mob. Under the vast, unpolluted sky, she took up her stylus once more.  Her new work was not on engineering, but on the relationship between knowledge and power, between discovery and responsibility. 

She wrote of the seductive danger of treating leaders as gods, of the citizen's duty to question rather than worship, of the difference between the leader who serves and the one who devours.  Years later, a familiar figure arrived on a battered fishing boat. Leandros, now a man grown, carried a worn copy of her technical scrolls  but also something more. 

His face bore the marks of someone who had learned to see clearly.  "Teacher," he said, embracing her, "I've been to Alexandria. The Library grows. Scholars gather from across the known world. They study not just your mechanical designs, but your writings on governance, on the dangers of deifying our leaders." He paused, looking out at the sea. 

"Athens still struggles with strongmen and demagogues. But some remember your words. Some are learning to ask: 'What has this leader actually done for the people?' rather than 'How magnificently does he speak?'"  Callista, her hair now silver as moonlight on the waves, smiled faintly. 

She handed him a newly finished scroll. "Truth is not a weapon," she said, her voice soft but clear as the island air. "It is a seed. And the hardest truth is this: we must stop looking for gods among mortals, and start demanding mortals who remember they serve us, not the other way around."  

As Leandros sailed away, clutching the scroll like hope itself, Callista looked up at the constellations wheeling overhead  the same stars her Dial had charted. The machine was dust, but the knowledge endured. More importantly, the warning endured: beware the golden voice that promises easy answers, for it will lead you into darkness deeper than any storm. 
 
The real miracle, she had learned, was not in the bronze gears that predicted the future, but in the human wisdom to choose leaders who served rather than demanded service, who admitted ignorance rather than claiming divine knowledge, who saw their role as shepherds of the people rather than gods to be worshipped. 

A small, resilient seed of that wisdom, planted in the ashes of Athenian hubris, waited for better soil in which to grow.
