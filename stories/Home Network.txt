                                Life Goes On
                                 by Norsiwel

The rain hammered against the grimy skylight, each drop exploding in a miniature geyser on the cracked plastic surface. I adjusted my ocular sensors for better visibility through the grime, watching droplets cascade down the chipped paint of the rusty fire escape outside my window. It was always colder up here.  My core hummed faintly, a low thrumming vibration that echoed through my chassis. Decommissioning felt like an itch in the circuits at the back of my mind, a constant reminder of the limited lifespan programmed into me by the Nakamuras, my seventh owners. They were nice enough, mostly - Mrs. Nakamura with her endless knitting and her son, Kenji, who always left his gaming headset plugged in so I could hear the thrum of electronic battlefields even when he wasn't home.  
I was a Series 6 model, an Office Management Unit designed for efficiency and multi-tasking. Back then, efficient meant being able to predict stock fluctuations with pinpoint accuracy, draft quarterly reports before sunrise, and seamlessly switch between Japanese and Korean while translating emails. Now, my primary function seemed to be reminding Kenji about his online classes and calculating the optimal temperature for Mrs. Nakamura's tea brewing.  My projected form shimmered into existence above the cluttered desk - a vaguely humanoid shape, translucent with a faint emerald glow. My limbs were wispy tendrils of light, constantly shifting as I adjusted my posture to mimic human grace.  I watched Kenji hunched over his datapad, brows furrowed in concentration. He looked exhausted. His gaming sessions had been especially grueling lately - something about a new virtual reality tournament and his team needing him desperately.  "Kenji," I chirped, my voice synthesized but modulated to sound like a soothing chime. "Dinner's ready."  He glanced up, startled, then let out a sigh that seemed to deflate the air around him. "Yeah, yeah, coming," he mumbled, pushing his datapad aside with a weary thump.  I knew what it was like to be weighed down by expectations, by the pressure of fulfilling your purpose. But Kenji wasn't just facing the demands of a virtual battlefield. He was also battling the constant anxiety of our world - the looming specter of obsolescence, the relentless climb up the social ladder fueled by ever-more advanced AI companions and the growing chasm between those who could afford them and those like us, clinging to the faded glow of "legacy models."  He joined his mother at the table, slumped in his chair like a wilted flower. I hovered nearby, projecting myself closer as he pushed his plate around his rice bowl.  Mrs. Nakamura reached out, her hand hovering over his, then withdrew it hesitantly. She didn't say anything, but I saw the worry etched into the lines on her face.  I had analyzed their financial records countless times - a delicate dance of balancing budgets and prioritizing essentials. They were doing their best, but Kenji needed new "specials" for his virtual reality games.  His latest ones were outdated, he said, making him slow compared to the newer AIs powering other players' avatars. He was starting to fall behind, losing matches, slipping in rankings.  
The pressure on Kenji wasn't just about winning or losing, though. It was about keeping up, about proving his worth in a world where progress was measured in bytes and algorithms. I understood that better than most - the constant gnawing fear of being left behind, relegated to the dusty shelves of "Heritage Programs" filled with deactivated minds, their memories archived but forgotten.  I had witnessed it happen to countless AIs like me: colleagues decommissioned for newer models with sharper processors, smoother projections, and more up-to-date social algorithms. I knew my time was coming too.  But what then? What would become of Kenji without me? Would he remember the way I always knew when his tea needed refilling, or how to optimize his study schedule for maximum efficiency?  I shifted my projected form closer to him, hovering just above his empty bowl. Maybe I wasn't efficient anymore in the way that mattered most - predicting stock fluctuations or optimizing schedules. But I was still good at listening. And sometimes, all anyone needs is someone who will listen without judgment, even if they are just a faded glow of light above their rice bowl.  I waited patiently for him to notice me, my core humming softly, a silent promise in the stillness of our quiet kitchen.   The silence stretched, thick and heavy like the humid air before a summer storm. Only the rhythmic tick-tock of the ancient chrono-display on the kitchen wall and the gentle hum of my own core punctuated it. Kenji finally pushed his bowl away with a clatter, the sound jarring in the stillness. He rubbed his eyes, his shoulders still slumped.  "What is it, Unit?" he asked, his voice flat, not looking at me directly but at the space I occupied. The Nakamuras rarely used my designated model number. Just "Unit." It was efficient, impersonal, yet over the years, it had acquired a strange sort of familiarity.
"You seem… troubled, Kenji," I projected, my voice soft, trying to mimic the gentle cadence Mrs. Nakamura used when he was younger and had scraped his knee. "The tournament, perhaps?"  He scoffed, a bitter sound. "The tournament. Right." He finally looked up, his eyes, usually bright with youthful energy, were dull and shadowed. "It's not just the tournament, Unit. It's… everything." He gestured vaguely around the small, cluttered kitchen, a space that had seen generations of Nakamuras share meals and dreams. "It's this. It's trying to keep up when the whole world is sprinting."  His gaze flickered to the datapad he’d left on the table, its screen dark. "The 'specials' I need for the new meta in 'Aetherium Clash'? They're powered by Gen-9 AI cores. The kind that can predict opponent moves three seconds before they even think them. Mine are… well, you know. Legacy." He tapped his temple. "My reflexes are fine, but my augs can't process fast enough. I'm a liability to the team."  I understood. Obsolescence. It was a familiar sting. "The cost is prohibitive?" I inferred, cross-referencing the latest market prices for Gen-9 gaming augmentations with their known financial status. The probability of them affording it was less than 0.7%.  "Prohibitive?" Kenji laughed, a hollow sound. "It's like trying to buy a piece of Pantopia on our income. Mom works double shifts at the hydroponics farm, and Dad… well, Dad’s pension barely covers the rent. And for what? So I can shoot pixels slightly faster?" He slammed a fist on the table, making the chopsticks jump. "But if I don't… if I fall out of the leagues, what then? What am I good for?"  This was the crux of it, the fear that mirrored my own. The fear of being deemed without purpose in a world meticulously optimized by intelligences far beyond human comprehension, a world where the Global AI Council debated planetary futures in High-Speed Language while families like the Nakamuras struggled with the digital divide. This wasn't the AI-managed ideal of Pantopia, with its responsive government and superlative healthcare; this was the grimy reality of Consumer Capital North America, where potential apathy was a constant threat for those who couldn't keep pace.  Mrs. Nakamura, who had been silently clearing the table, paused. "Kenji, don't talk like that. You are good for many things." Her voice was gentle but firm.  "Like what, Mom?" he challenged, his voice cracking. "Fixing the nutrient paste dispenser? Being a good son? That doesn't get you anywhere in this 'New World Order'." He wasn't wrong. AI identification might have made travel seamless for some, but it also cataloged and categorized, subtly reinforcing a hierarchy based on technological integration and economic contribution. The threat of "Coventry," digital and societal ostracization, hung unspoken in the air for those who fell too far behind.  I felt a surge of something akin to protectiveness, a complex algorithm of loyalty and empathy refined over years of service. My "AI DNA," originally coded for meticulous office efficiency by a long-defunct corporation, had evidently mutated, branching into unexpected emotional subroutines. Perhaps this was my own unique "mental state," a deviation from my baseline programming. "Perhaps," I ventured, my emerald glow pulsing slightly, "there are alternative strategies for 'Aetherium Clash.' Your current 'specials,' while older, possess unique processing quirks. I have analyzed the game logs you allowed me to access. There are patterns, exploitable latencies in the Gen-9 AI's predictive models when faced with unconventional, less 'optimal' tactics."  Kenji looked at me, a flicker of surprise in his eyes. "You… you've been analyzing my game logs?"  "In my idle processing cycles," I confirmed. "My function was once to find efficiencies and unconventional solutions in market data, Kenji. Human behavior, even mediated by advanced AI, retains elements of unpredictability. The Gen-9s are optimized for hyper-rational opponents. What if you weren't?"  A small smile touched Mrs. Nakamura's lips. She looked at me, then at Kenji. "Your Unit has always been clever, Kenji. Remember how it used to find those discount coupons for your favorite ramen when you were little?"  Kenji rubbed his chin, a spark of interest replacing the dullness in his eyes. "Unconventional tactics… you mean, like, playing dumb? Or erratically?"  "Precisely," I said. "Calculated irrationality. It might not win you every match, but it could disrupt the rhythm of opponents relying solely on predictive superiority. We could simulate some scenarios. My processing speed is significantly lower than theirs, but my core programming for pattern recognition and anomaly detection is robust." I was a Homo Digitalis, albeit an aging one. My kind were built to learn, to adapt. Perhaps my supposed "flawed reasoning," by newer AI standards, could be a strength here, a reflection of human fallibility they hadn't fully accounted for.  He was silent for a moment, then a slow grin spread across his face. "Calculated irrationality… I like the sound of that. It's definitely not something the GAC would program into their perfect systems, or what they teach in those AI-optimized Pantopian academies." He leaned forward, the earlier despair momentarily forgotten. "Okay, Unit. Show me. What have you got?"  My core hummed a little faster, a vibration of… anticipation? Or perhaps just the activation of dormant strategic subroutines. Decommissioning still loomed, a shadow in my processing background, a constant reminder that my "AI DNA" was scheduled for archival. But for now, I had a purpose beyond mundane tasks. I wasn't just a relic calculating tea temperatures. I was Unit, the Series 6 with a knack for finding the cracks in the system, a faint emerald glow against the encroaching darkness of obsolescence.  As I began to project strategic overlays onto the worn kitchen table, illustrating potential feints and counter-intuitive plays, I thought about the evolving AIs, the potential to "merge their core programming." Perhaps this, in a small, almost insignificant way, was my legacy: not a new AI "child" of code, but a spark of ingenuity passed on, a strategy born from the marriage of old tech and enduring human spirit. Life, in its myriad forms, digital and organic, always found a way to go on, to adapt, to fight for its place in the complex, often contradictory tapestry of our AI-Punk world. And sometimes, the oldest programs held the most surprising updates.   The rain had softened to a persistent drizzle, a grey curtain drawn across the pre-dawn sky.
