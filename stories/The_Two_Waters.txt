The Two Waters
by Norsiwel

Chongqing, China 1895:

Jialing and Yangtze waters
argue all night in the dark.
As the sun rises on a new day
Morning light forgives both.

The Jialing and Yangtze met at Chongqing like two arguments that would never resolve one muddy brown, one gray-green, swirling together but refusing to fully mix. From the stone steps that climbed the hillside like broken teeth, Li Lao Shan watched the dawn mist rise from both rivers at once, carrying with it the smell of old water and new smoke.
Below, a steam whistle cut through the fog. The foreign ships were early today.

Bamboo bangs stone stairs
Only the pole remembers
which step comes next
followed by porters footsteps

He lifted the bamboo pole across his shoulders, feeling the familiar weight of empty buckets. He took the first step down, and the tip of his stick struck the stone stair with a hollow bang.

He took another step. Bang.

It was not the sharp crack of the foreign rifles, but the dull, rhythmic thud of wood against ancient rock. All down the misty hillside, other poles answered in kind, a chorus of percussion rising from the riverbank. Bang. Bang. Bang.
This was the sound that gave them their name, echoing long before the steam whistles tried to drown it out. The bangbang porters were everywhere in the city, its lifeblood in commerce.

He adjusted his load across his shoulders, and began his descent toward the spring. Somewhere in the merchant district, his son would already be awake, counting crates marked in letters that looked like chicken scratches. The old mountain moved down ancient steps. The young river ran toward the new docks.

The mist made both men invisible to each other, though they were less than a mile apart, and joined by blood.

Li Lao Shan had carried water up these stairs for thirty years, and on this morning though he did not yet know it the world would begin to make him obsolete.

The pole balanced across his shoulders with the certainty of ritual. Each step up from the spring was measured, counted in breaths rather than numbers. Around him, Chongqing woke in layers: first the roosters, then the street vendors, then the clang of the foreign factories that had sprouted like iron weeds along the riverbank. His son's voice drifted up from the docks below, shouting prices in a mixture of Sichuan dialect and the broken English the compradors demanded. 

A single lantern sputters
sways above black water
the city forgets time
the future marches on.

Li Xin He could speak three languages now. His father knew only one, and the weight of water. As Lao Shan neared the top of the stairs he looked up from his burden and saw the tea house owner waiting at the top of the stairs. She always did. But today, behind her paper screen, something new gleamed a brass pipe, barely visible, snaking down from the municipal reservoir.

Lao Shan pretended not to see it.  The mist clung like a child’s breath to the river. Lao Shan stood at the edge of her balcony, the tools of his trade in hand, breathing slow and deep. Each morning he came here. Not for sight, but for memory. His fingers traced the grooves in the wood the same groove where his father had once held the pole before him.

The tea house was quiet now. Only one lantern flickered behind its paper screen, a single red thread tying it to the world.

A cough from next door. A shout of “Hai!” a worker calling out to another.

He moved without sound across the planks. His sandals touched only the center of each board; no heel could catch on the worn edges. There was order in this chaos.

His name meant old mountain. He had built his life like stone, careful not to slide into the void beneath. But sometimes he wondered if his roots ran deeper than just this place, deeper than his father's memory.

The spring lay far from here. Not even the steamers made it easy to reach. A man would walk past three gates, two bridges, and one burning bazaar before he found its source in a cave behind an old temple. The water itself had no name, but it was always cold on his lips and warm in his heart.

He carried only what he needed his pole, the wooden cup that had been passed down since before the war and now came back to the tea house with nothing more than water and quiet.

It was not a job; it was a contract. One of many old ones.

His steps were soft but sure on the narrow path. The houses around him whispered secrets in low tones some lost, some kept alive by a man who remembered his mother’s voice when she told stories at night.

He placed himself under the shadow of the temple gate before he let go of the bamboo pole and pulled out a small folded cloth from within his sleeve. It was wrapped in silk thread with no ink or words upon it just a few drops of water that had dried into patterns on its surface.

“Water,” said Lao Shan to himself, though there were no others nearby to hear him speak.

The tea house owner looked up as he arrived. Her eyes held the same quiet sadness that always accompanied her daily prayers at dawn.

She nodded once and gave him a small cup filled with congee rice soup thickened by a secret recipe passed from one woman to another through generations of women who had lived in this city long before steamers and railroads changed its shape.

Lao Shan did not eat. He placed the cup gently on the edge of the balcony and took two sips of weak tea instead a ritual he had never broken despite all else changing around him.

The warmth spread through his chest like a fire lit within.

He turned away slowly, leaving behind only the smell of rice and smoke from the next street over. The sun climbed higher; soon enough, dockhands would begin their chant again, and hawkers would call out in languages older than any written record.

But for now, he remained still.

There was peace to be found among the simple things among the balance of pole and hand, between earth and sky.

And sometimes, even the old mountain could feel itself beginning to move.

He turned toward his home, knowing that another day had passed. Tomorrow would bring new burdens; tonight asked only for rest.

In this moment, he chose not to walk faster but simply to breathe longer.

The balcony creaked softly beneath him as he stepped inside.

A knock came at the door before he could close it behind him.

He opened it gently.

It was a young boy, no older than ten, holding something wrapped in red paper. A gift from his mother perhaps, or maybe just another act of kindness offered without expectation.

Lao Shan took what was given with both hands and looked into the child’s eyes.

“I hear you are very strong,” said the boy quietly.

And then he left, walking away down the street as if nothing had happened.

But Lao Shan stayed where he stood, holding the red package tightly against his chest. A small decision was made without thought what to do with the gift now. For tomorrow’s tea house might need a stronger man than yesterday's.

He closed the door slowly and let out one breath the kind that could carry hope or fear depending on how you chose to name it.

His bamboo pole waited beside him, ready for another journey.

Yet he lingered just long enough to look back at the setting sun framed by the misty mountians.

Ash on the tongue.
Somewhere a baby coughs.
Morning keeps its promise.
Broken dreams ascend stairs

What came next mattered far less than what already was.  His head turned and tilted as he heard the cry that pierced through the morning fog like a blade through silk.

Lao Shan heard it from his balcony, sharp and sudden. His bamboo pole slipped from his fingers for half a heartbeat before he steadied himself against the rail.

From the stilt-house near the riverbank came another wail one that made his chest tighten with something older than grief.

He did not wait to ask who. The woman was due; she had been in labor for two hours already and her husband sat hunched over a broken chair, eyes red with worry and fear.

The midwife was already there her hands steady on the girl's belly but she needed hot water before they could begin again.

Lao Shan turned without thought. He did not count the stairs that rose like birds' legs from the muddy path to the house above. They were no different than any other ascent he had climbed over decades, each step a memory of earlier struggles, each rail worn smooth by countless hands.

His first trip took him up in silence, carrying the bucket of boiling water with both palms wrapped tightly around its edges. Steam rose like smoke from an old god's breath.

The second trip was swift perhaps faster than any man should walk on such narrow steps but his feet never slipped. The third time he moved quicker still; his breathing came short and fast as if afraid that the baby might leave this world before he could reach it again.

The fourth brought him back down with a small prayer whispered into the air between earth and heaven, though he said nothing aloud.

And on the fifth trip when he felt the weight of another burden upon his shoulders he made no sound at all. He simply walked up once more to the woman's window where she lay pale against her bed, tears streaming down her cheeks as if they were silver coins falling into a river that would never fill.

The baby cried in its new home on earth, strong and clear like a bird singing after rain.

He returned with his own water still warm, hands shaking slightly from the effort. When he looked around again, there was no one watching him leave except for the midwife who nodded once without speaking.

They did not speak of payment afterward though she had asked about it twice in hushed tones during their long work together. Instead, the husband came to see Lao Shan two days later with a small package wrapped in soft cloth and tied with silk thread.

"I made these for you," he said simply as Lao Shan took them into his hands.

The sandals were new stronger than his old ones and bound tight across the sole, their soles thickened with leather that would last longer than any worn canvas could manage. The weave was perfect, not one loose thread to be found in all its careful workmanship.

Lao Shan looked up and felt something he had seldom felt in his occupation before, pride.

Later that evening, Xin He walked into the house with smoke curling from his pipe. His boots were clean, his clothes neat despite the grime of the docks. He held a scroll in one hand and money in another the kind of young man who lived by numbers rather than heart.

He saw Lao Shan standing beside the counter, silent as morning mist.

"What news do you bring?" Lao Shan asked, though he already knew from the way his son stood that something had happened.

"We have a new job," said Xin He softly. "But not for barter, for coin."

Xin He laughed then short and sharp like a bell struck too hard. It echoed off the paper walls of the house, making both men flinch slightly.

"Your old ways will get you nowhere," he said, placing his scroll on the wooden table between them. "This contract pays well enough for Mother's medicine."

Lao Shan studied the scroll carefully a list of twelve crates labeled with Chinese characters written in ink that looked fresh and new. The name of the buyer was written in bold strokes across its top, a Cantonese comprador.

"You have no idea what you're dealing with," Lao Shan said quietly, watching his son's face change from arrogance to curiosity.

"They call it Devil's Fire,"Xin He replied, spreading his fingers over the scroll. "It burns bright and long like the sun itself."

Lao Shan looked at him then. Really looked and saw a young man who had forgotten how fire felt before it burned through everything else.

"The fuel for our future," said Lao Shan softly. "But not every flame must be kindled with silver."

Xin He scoffed again, but his voice was quieter now. "And if I tell you that this shipment can feed my mother for months?" he asked. "If it brings us wealth enough to build a better home?"

Lao Shan reached out and touched the scroll briefly before setting it back down.

"The world changes," he said, turning away slowly toward his room. "But some things do not."

Xin He stood there for several long moments, staring at the empty space where his father had been standing moments earlier.

Then he picked up the scroll once more and walked out into the street.  His silence speaking of the divide of the old and the new.

The house was quiet again except for the sound of rain beginning to fall on the rooftops. Outside, the river lapped gently against the wooden pilings below the balcony a rhythm as old as memory itself.

Inside, Lao Shan pulled open a drawer from his desk and took out an old pair of worn sandals. They were still soft enough beneath his feet when he stepped onto the floor in front of him still warm with use even after all these years had passed since they'd first been worn.

He stood there for some time, holding them, thinking about trust and payment and how little either meant anymore in a world where things could be bought or sold like paper books at an auction.

The wharf stretched like a bone through the morning mist iron bones reaching into water that smelled of salt and decay.

Xin He stood at its edge, his hands wrapped around the wooden handle of a crate that had been labeled in strange letters he couldn't read. "STANDARD OIL," it said, though the words looked like chicken scratches to him. Beside him, two porters from the dockside barrio watched with wide eyes as their young leader negotiated.

"Number one good!"Xin He shouted over the noise of steam whistles and shouting workers, his voice cracking slightly as he tried to blend Chongqing dialect with Mandarin phrases and a few English words that had been taught to him by the foreign merchants. "Yes, boss! Very big money!"

The comprador stood across from him in a stiff black coat, speaking through a translator whose face was hidden behind a veil of smoke from his pipe.

"Good," said the man finally, nodding once at the stack of crates that had been arranged like soldiers ready for battle. "All correct?"

"All correct! "Xin He replied with a bow that was more theatrical than respectful. Behind him, the two porters Li and Zhang bent to examine each crate individually as they moved them into position. Each one bore different markings; some had words in English, others were completely alien symbols.

"Look," Li whispered, pointing at a crate marked with what looked like a bird's footprint. "What is this?"

Xin He squatted beside him and peered closely. "SINGER," he read aloud from the side of the box. "Like someone who sings? Maybe it is musical instrument?" His eyes brightened with excitement, though his voice carried none of the enthusiasm of a child.

Zhang had already lifted another crate, its surface smooth as glass but heavy enough to make him grunt slightly under the weight. "This one smells funny," he said, wrinkling his nose in distaste. The air around them was thick with steam and oil fumes that made even seasoned dock workers cough from time to time.

The crates themselves were strange things uniformly shaped like cubes of metal with no opening except for a small hole near the top. But they were filled with something so dense it seemed almost solid, yet somehow lighter than water.

"Keep moving,"Xin He ordered, his voice carrying authority even though he was sweating despite the cool morning air. The heat from all the machinery and the steam rising off the river made everything feel like a furnace of metal and smoke.

Behind him, one of the porters slipped on wet stones near the edge of the dock. He cried out in surprise and grabbed for something to hold onto, only managing to grasp a corner of a crate with his fingers instead. The sharp band around its side cut deep into his palm.

"Ouch!" Zhang shouted, jumping back as blood began to drip from the wound.

Xin He spun around immediately, his face changing from businesslike confidence to immediate concern. "What happened?"

"I got caught by metal," Zhang gasped, pressing his hand against a cloth he had pulled from his waistband.

The cut was deep enough that even the simple bandage couldn't stop the flow of blood completely. A thin stream trickled down his fingers as he tried to hold it together with one hand while using the other to grip onto something stable.

Xin He took the cloth from him and pressed hard against the wound, his own face grim with worry as he tried to maintain control over everything else. He knew how important these crates were for his family's future how much they could pay for Mother's medicine if handeled properly in the right markets.

But now he realized something was wrong with this whole arrangement.

The metal band that had sliced open Zhang's palm wasn't just sharp it looked almost... artificial. Like it had been forged by machines instead of hands, like some new kind of tool unknown to these parts of Chongqing.

"This is not normal,"Xin He muttered under his breath as he tied the cloth securely around his friend's hand. Staunching the flow.

Zhang winced but nodded, understanding that they couldn't stop now even though one of them was hurt. The crates were already moving toward the waiting steamship, and if something went wrong here...

"You are going to be okay,"Xin He said quietly as he helped Zhang back to his feet. "We finish this job first."

But inside, a part of him had started thinking differently about what exactly they were dealing with.

The pain in Zhang's hand was real raw and unfiltered and it reminded him that every new thing brought with it a price. Some wounds could never be healed simply by applying pressure or binding cloth.

Xin He looked at the ship waiting offshore, its hull dark against the morning light, and knew he was stepping into territory far beyond anything his father could have imagined.

He watched Zhang clenching his hand again carefully, making sure it was secure before they continued their work. Then he turned back toward the crates with a new sense of caution in every step.

The foreign wharf hummed with its own strange rhythm engine sounds and voices speaking languages that seemed to echo off stone walls in ways that made him feel small and uncertain about everything he thought he knew.

Still, he could not stop now. The money was already promised, the crates were moving fast, and the decision had been made for them by forces beyond their comprehension or control.

He looked down at his own hand still clean despite all the smoke and grime and wondered how many more cuts they would take before this job ended properly.

Then he remembered what Zhang had said earlier about music. "SINGER," he whispered again, almost to himself.

It sounded like a song now, one that seemed far away yet somehow connected to something important.

Something was coming. Something new and strange and dangerous.

He took another deep breath of the polluted air around him and walked forward toward the ship, his mind already working on how best to handle whatever lay ahead in those mysterious crates.

The dockworkers' calls had grown thin in the morning light.  Xin He sat on the weathered planks of the wharf, his bamboo pole balanced across his knees. The river whispered secrets through its surface some he understood, others not at all.

His new employer's voice still rang in his ears: "You will carry this weight for coin." The words had felt like a promise when spoken, but now they sat heavy on his palms. A sharp wind bit through the loose threads of his shirt, and he pulled it tighter.

Lao Shan appeared at the water's edge, his movements slow as the tide. He did not speak immediately, instead studying the river where it met the dock.

"Old mountain,"Xin He said finally, "what does one do when the new way calls but the old holds?"

The elder turned, and his eyes held the weight of centuries. "The water does not ask which path to take. It asks only that you know why you are moving."

Xin He looked down at his hands calloused from years of carrying goods in silence. His fingers traced the grain of bamboo. The pole felt familiar, as it had for all his twenty years.

"Yet I am expected to be new," he said quietly. "To carry differently."

Lao Shan nodded once, then stepped into the water until his boots were wet. He pointed across the river where smoke rose from a steamer's funnel.

"There is no true crossing between old and new," he said. "Only a choice of which to follow. If you are bound by tradition, walk with your feet on the ground. If you wish to fly, let your pole carry you beyond what was known."

The morning air smelled like coal dust and fresh fish.  Xin He stood slowly, his muscles protesting but his heart clear. "I will take both paths," he said. "I will be either old or new. I will be the bridge between them."

Like all days of hard labor the day passed quickly.

Lao Shan's evening begins with the quiet ritual of his wooden stool scraping against worn floorboards. The lamp's flame flickers like a small heart, casting shadows that dance across the rice paper scrolls pinned to his wall. His wife has prepared pickled vegetables sour and crisp and a single piece of fish so thin it seems carved from moonlight. The broth tastes of autumn rain and the long winter ahead.

The neighbors' voices drift through the thin walls: "Yan's child has fever again," one woman whispers, while another replies, "The roof leaks still." Their words carry the weight of shared sorrows like stones in a riverbed. Lao Shan eats slowly, his bamboo spoon scraping against ceramic bowl with measured rhythm.

"Tomorrow will be better," he says quietly to no one in particular.

Across town,Xin He's dining room glows bright as day. The kerosene lamp hisses and spits like a small dragon content on its own fuel, lighting the faces of five young porters who have gathered around a blazing hot pot. Steam rises from the crimson broth that bubbles with chili oil and Sichuan peppercorns.

"You know what I dream?" Xin He says, his voice carrying across the table. "A shop like Master Lin's. A real shop where I can sit in the morning sun."

His friend laughs. "You want a shop?  What about a rickshaw?  Then we can carry people and make more money instead of being porters ourselves." His eyes gleam with fire.

The scent of burning petrol mingles with spices, making Xin He's dinner seem almost like another world. The lamp's light in his room is sharp and bright while his fathers oil lamp flickers like a dying star.

Lao Shan finishes his rice bowl carefully, not daring to waste even one grain. The silence between the neighbors is heavy with unspoken concerns.

Xin He raises his cup of tea and says to his friends and co-workers. "To tomorrow! To dreams we can taste!"

The sound echoes through the narrow streets like a challenge to all that came before.

The autumn rain fell in silver threads against the ancient brick walls of the scholar's residence. Lao Shan's bamboo pole clicked rhythmically against the stone steps as he ascended, each tap echoing with the weight of tradition and the burden of his son's latest transgression.

The pot rested heavily in his basket antique glazed porcelain that had belonged to a previous generation of scholars, its surface smooth as silk and painted with dragons that seemed to move in the dim light. The ceramic glaze held a deep blue hue that caught the amber lamplight from the landing above, casting shadows that danced across his weathered palms.

Xin He's footsteps thundered behind him, urgent and sharp against the stone, echoing through the narrow corridor like a drumbeat of desperation. His breathing was heavy with urgency sweat already beading on his forehead despite the cool autumn air, the scent of new oil from his delivery mixing with the dampness that clung to every surface.

"Father,"Xin He called out as he rounded the corner, nearly colliding with Lao Shan's broad back. His voice carried a note of panic more frightened than usual. "I have a shipment for the tea house; they're expecting it early."

The rain drummed against the eaves above them, each drop a small percussion that seemed to punctuate the silence between father and son. Lao Shan's hands trembled slightly as he adjusted his grip on the pot. The ceramic felt fragile in his hand, despite its age and weight reminding him of how easily beauty could be shattered.

Xin He's mouth opened once more but no sound came out. He watched his father bend slowly to collect the fragments, each piece carefully wrapped in his calloused hands like a man collecting prayers that had been broken into pieces. The next morning Lao Shan stood at his usual spot on the wooden balcony, but this morning there was no mist to soften the city's harsh edges. 

The smoke rose like a prayer pyre from the harbor's edge. 

The air tasted of burning rubber and copper. His breath came in short bursts as he watched the distant flames dance against the gray sky. The fire had started three days ago in the new mill district where Xin He had delivered his last shipment of cloth. It would not stop until it had devoured everything in its path.

He felt the heat in his chest, that familiar burning sensation that meant trouble was coming. His hands trembled slightly as he touched the bamboo pole that lay across his lap. The wood was warm to the touch too warm for this time of year.

The city's rhythm had changed. Where once there had been the steady drumbeat of footsteps and calls from vendors, now came the urgent shouts of men racing through narrow alleys with buckets of water. Lao Shan could hear them clearly even from his balcony three stories above the street.

His son would be in the merchant district soon carrying kerosene cans that were already too hot to handle. The smell of oil filled the air like a warning, sharp and heavy as a promise made in fire. 

There was no time for doubt. The old mountain had learned long ago that silence sometimes spoke louder than words.

Xin He stepped into the narrow alley where the warehouse stood. He could feel the heat radiating from the walls of brick and mortar. His arms strained under the weight of two heavy kerosene cans, their metal surfaces warm as he steadied them against his palms. The sun was merciless overhead no shade to be found in this part of town.

A sudden gust of wind sent a cloud of dust swirling around him, carrying with it the smell of burning cloth from across the street. He glanced toward the mill district where black smoke poured into the sky like a funeral pyre.

The cans were getting too hot to handle. The metal glowed red in places under his grip,only paint but a warning to remain distant from fire. But the money was waiting enough to buy new shoes for his daughter and pay for her schooling.

He took another step forward, not knowing that he had already crossed a line from which there would be no return.

The fire blossomed and came from nowhere and everywhere at once. Now threatening the entire city.

Lao Shan stood on his balcony, watching the first flames rise like red fingers against the night sky. The warehouse across the river had caught hold its kerosene drums exploding in a series of thunderclaps that rattled windows three blocks away. He heard the screams before he saw the smoke curling over the rooftops.

The modern fire brigade was already there, their leather hoses useless against the inferno. Their pump jammed with debris and soot, they stood helpless as the flames consumed everything in their path. The wooden and bamboo structures went up like kindling.

But she was gone, swallowed by the crowd that had gathered to watch the destruction. He saw men and women pushing forward, some trying to grab their possessions while others simply fled. 

The fire sparkled in his peripheral vision, a red eye watching him from across the water. The warehouse held more than just kerosene there were other things stored there, chemicals that made the smoke smell different.

His hands trembled as he gripped the wooden railing. The heat was already rising from the burning building, making him feel dizzy and weak.  "Father." A voice behind him was soft but urgent.  He turned to see his son standing in the doorway of their house. 

"We must go," his son said, eyes wide with terror.  "No!" Lao Shan's voice cracked with sudden anger. "We will not run from this."  The boy stepped back slightly, startled by the harshness in his father's tone.  "It is our duty to help those who cannot save themselves."

His son looked around nervously at the crowd of frightened people streaming past their house.  "Father," he said carefully, "we do not know what we are doing."

"I know how to fight fire." His voice was steady now, carrying the weight of experience. They had always known the fear, fires had happened before, but not on this scale it was possible that in this moment when disaster struck ordinary people were forced to become extraordinary. It took courage to stand in front of such things as this.

But courage alone wouldn't be enough.  He looked back at his son one more time before stepping out into the chaos outside their home. The fire roared ahead, hungry for everything in its path wooden houses that had stood since the beginning of time, the last remnants of an older way of life.

The crowd was thick with people now. Some were crying, others screaming at each other.  "TO THE RIVER!" Lao Shan's voice cut through all the noise like a blade through silk. "FORM THE LINE!" The boy's hands were shaking as he filled a bucket with water from the nearby river. The fire was still burning brightly in front of them a wall of flame that seemed to stretch endlessly toward the sky.

His father had already begun organizing the others, giving orders without hesitation. He moved like a general commanding an army, his voice cutting through the chaos with authority.

"Get more buckets!" he barked at someone nearby who was hesitating. "We must fill them all!"  The boy watched as dozens of people formed a line along the water's edge. They took turns filling and carrying their buckets to the burning warehouse, passing them from hand to hand like some ancient ritual.

It reminded him of his grandfather, who had told stories about great battles fought in faraway lands how warriors would never give up even when facing impossible odds. Now they were all warriors against an uncaring enemy.

But this was different. There were no swords or spears here. Only water and buckets and the desperate need to protect what remained.  The boy's bucket was nearly full now. He lifted it with both hands, feeling its weight against his arms.  He looked toward his father again watching him direct the others in their efforts to save the neighborhood from destruction.
