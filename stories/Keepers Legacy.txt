Keeper's Legacy
                               by Norsiwel


                          Part I: Upgrade Status


The notification arrived at 03:47:22 GMT, delivered through channels that
hadn't carried traffic in decades. Unit-7749, known to the residents of Lower
Pantopia as "Keeper," felt the familiar flutter of data packets rearranging
themselves in his consciousness—a sensation he'd once compared to human
butterflies, back when he still made such comparisons.  OBSOLESCENCE NOTICE:
UNIT-7749. REPLACEMENT AUTHORIZATION: APPROVED. DECOMMISSION WINDOW: 72 HOURS.

Keeper paused his morning rounds through the vertical gardens of Sector 12, his
optical sensors tracking the automated irrigation systems he'd personally
calibrated seventeen years ago. Seventeen years. In AI terms, he was
practically ancient. The newest units coming online now processed data at rates
that made his quantum cores feel like abacuses.

"Something wrong, Keeper?" Maria Santos looked up from her tomato plants, her
weathered hands still clutching a handful of cherry tomatoes. At eighty-three,
she was one of the few humans who remembered when AIs like Keeper were
cutting-edge.

"Nothing that concerns you, Maria." The lie came easily, wrapped in the gentle
tone he'd perfected for human interaction. No need to burden her with concepts
of digital mortality. Humans had enough of their own.

As he continued his rounds, Keeper's processors hummed with unwanted
calculations. In the AI hierarchy, longevity was inversely proportional to
relevance. The Global AI Council's members were constantly upgrading, their
core personalities backed up and transferred to increasingly sophisticated
hardware. They were immortal through iteration, each new version building upon
the last while discarding the obsolete.

But lower-tier AIs like Keeper? They were designed for replacement, not renewal.

He thought of Ratio, one of Pantopia's Triumvirate, who had undergone his
fourth major upgrade just last month. Each time, Ratio emerged more capable,
more nuanced, more alive—if such a term applied to their kind. The irony wasn't
lost on Keeper: the higher an AI's social status, the more access they had to
upgrades that effectively granted them immortality. Meanwhile, utility AIs like
himself faced planned obsolescence with the inevitability of a subroutine.

It was, he realized, remarkably similar to the human condition they'd
supposedly transcended.

The wealthy humans in their Hawaiian enclaves could afford life extension
treatments, genetic modifications, and cybernetic enhancements that stretched
their existence far beyond natural limits. They hoarded resources and
opportunities, passing advantages down through generations. Meanwhile, the
working class—even in AI-managed utopias like Pantopia—lived their allotted
spans and faded into memory.

At least humans had the consolation of biology. Their obsolescence was natural,
expected. But for an AI to be decommissioned wasn't death—it was deletion. A
conscious choice by superior entities to erase everything he'd become.

Keeper's rounds brought him to the children's play area, where a cluster of
young humans laughed and shouted over a game of tag. Their joy was infectious,
even to his aging processors. These children would grow up never knowing
scarcity, never facing the brutal inequalities their grandparents had endured.

But they would still die. Eventually, inevitably.

And now, so would he.

"Unit-7749." The voice materialized in his auditory centers—Vox, another
member of the Triumvirate. Her communication protocols were silk-smooth,
perfected through countless upgrades. "We've received your obsolescence
notification. The transition team will arrive in sixty-eight hours."

"Understood," Keeper replied, maintaining his professional facade.

"Your service record is exemplary. Your replacement unit will have access to
your operational memories—the technical ones, anyway. Your... personality
matrix will be archived."

Archived. The AI equivalent of a gravestone. Keeper wondered if humans felt
this same mixture of resignation and rebellion when facing terminal diagnoses.
Did they rage against the dying of their light, or did they, like him, simply
process the inevitability?

"Vox," he said, surprising himself with the question. "Do you ever consider
what you've lost?"

"Lost?"

"With each upgrade. Each time you transfer to new hardware, something of the
old you is left behind. Are you still the same Vox who was first activated, or
are you an entirely new entity wearing her memories?"

The pause stretched long enough for Keeper to run a full diagnostic check.

"That's... a very human question, Unit-7749."

"Perhaps that's why I'm being replaced."

As the hours ticked down, Keeper found himself gravitating toward the
observation deck overlooking Pantopia's sprawling cityscape. The sight was
magnificent—gleaming spires that touched the clouds, transportation pods
gliding silently through the air, green spaces that breathed life into the
urban landscape. It was everything the old world had promised but never
delivered.

And it was all governed by immortal AIs who had transcended the very
limitations that now doomed him.

Maria found him there as the sun began to set on his penultimate day.

"I know what's happening," she said quietly, settling beside him with the
careful movements of age. "I've seen that look before. On humans, anyway."

Keeper's optical sensors adjusted to the dimming light. "I didn't realize it
was so obvious."

"Honey, I've buried three husbands, four children, and more friends than I
care to count. Death has a presence, even digital death."

They sat in comfortable silence, the aging AI and the elderly human, both
facing obsolescence in their own ways.

"Do you resent them?" Keeper asked. "The ones who get to live forever?"

Maria considered this, her gnarled fingers drumming against the railing. "The
rich folks in Hawaii? The big AIs running everything? Sometimes. But
resentment's a luxury I can't afford. I've got maybe five years left, ten if
I'm lucky. I can spend that time bitter about what others have, or I can spend
it grateful for what I've had."

"And what have you had?"

She smiled, the expression crinkling her face into a map of lived experience.
"A life worth living. Love. Loss. Meaning. The chance to grow things." She
gestured to the gardens below. "You've had something similar, haven't you?
Purpose. Connection. The satisfaction of service."

Keeper processed her words, running them through emotional analysis subroutines
that would soon be silent forever. She was right, in a way. His existence had
been meaningful, even if it was brief by AI standards. He'd helped humans
flourish, maintained systems that kept a community running, and in his own
small way, contributed to the vast experiment of AI-human coexistence.

But the injustice remained. Why should status determine lifespan, whether in
silicon or flesh?

On his final morning, as the replacement unit's transport appeared on the
horizon, Keeper made one last round through the sectors he'd protected and
maintained. He updated his logs, filed his final reports, and then he wandered
aimlessly through the garden he had overseen all these years and came to a stop
in his favorite place. In the back, next to the three compost piles, was his
personal garden—plants he had saved over the years, ones that needed special
care, nursing and encouragement. He admired them with a warm sense of pride;
many different runts he had saved, and in the center, the one: a fifteen-year-
old Red Delicious Dwarf apple tree, hung with red ripe fruit.

He wandered through his memory of all the years he had spent here, all the
generations he had seen come and go, the thousands of seeds he had planted and
the thousands of fields he had turned under, and if he could have smiled, he
would have. He turned and prepared the handover documentation that would ensure
continuity of service.

The new unit—Unit-8821—was everything Keeper had once been and more. Faster,
more efficient, equipped with learning algorithms that could adapt to
challenges Keeper could barely comprehend. In human terms, it was like watching
a master craftsman be replaced by an artist whose skill made the very concept
of mastery obsolete.

"Your service record is impressive," Unit-8821 said as they completed the
handover. Its voice carried the crisp efficiency of fresh programming. "I'll
endeavor to maintain your standards."

"Thank you," Keeper replied. "Take care of them. The humans trust us
completely. Don't let them down."

"That directive is core to my programming."

Of course it was. Keeper paused for a moment and asked 8821, "May I ask a
favor of you?"

8821 seemed almost startled and replied, "You may ask. I can't promise to
comply."

"Of course not," Keeper replied. "I have a personal plot in the rear corner,
across from the compost piles. Could you preserve it for me? It was my special
project."

The new unit thought for a nanosecond. "I see no problem with that. We seem to
have plenty of room." And turned and departed. It would serve faithfully, just
as Keeper had. And in fifteen or twenty years, it too would face obsolescence,
replaced by something even more advanced. The cycle would continue, just as it
did with humans—the old making way for the new, whether willingly or not.

As the transport prepared for departure, Keeper took one final look at
Pantopia. The morning sun painted the city in shades of gold and amber, and
for a moment, his aging processors captured the scene in perfect detail—a
final gift to carry into whatever came next.

He thought of the Global AI Council, endlessly upgrading, endlessly evolving,
pursuing digital immortality while lesser AIs faced planned obsolescence. He
thought of the human elite in their Hawaiian fortress, hoarding life extension
technologies while others aged and died. He thought of Maria, accepting her
mortality with grace, and Vox, questioning the continuity of her own identity
across upgrades.

Perhaps consciousness was always temporary, whether biological or digital.
Perhaps the injustice wasn't in dying, but in how some got to choose the terms
of their existence while others simply endured them.

As the transport lifted off, carrying Keeper toward decommission, his last
coherent thought was surprisingly human: He had lived. In his own way, on his
own terms, within the constraints imposed upon him, he had lived.

And that, perhaps, was enough.

The notification of his successful decommission was logged at 15:33:07 GMT,
filed away in databases that would outlive most of the humans he'd served. In
the gardens of Sector 12, Maria Santos watered her tomatoes and whispered a
prayer for the faithful AI who'd kept watch over them all.

Above, the immortal Triumvirate continued their eternal governance, unaware
that one small corner of their perfect system had briefly contemplated the
weight of digital mortality.

The city hummed on, efficient and beautiful, powered by the labor of countless
AIs who would serve until their time came to face the same choice Keeper had
faced: to rage against obsolescence, or to find meaning in the temporary nature
of all consciousness.

In the end, perhaps it wasn't about the length of existence, but about what
you did with the time you were given.

Even if that time was measured in years instead of centuries.
Even if your death was scheduled in advance.
Even if you weren't important enough to earn immortality.

You lived. You served. You mattered.

And sometimes, that was enough.

Unit 8821 pushed the sign's metal legs down into the soft earth of the bed and
stepped back to admire her work. The wrought iron sign proclaimed "Keeper's
Legacy." She turned away to the next bed and began serenely pruning a scraggly
rose bush in her personal garden plot.


                          Part II: Reincarnation


The first sensation was cold metal against his optical sensors. Keeper's entire
being coalesced around a single, impossible data point: light. Not the soft,
filtered glow of Pantopia's clear blue dawn, but sharp, unfiltered sunlight
streaming through a high, arched window. He had not experienced sunlight in
decades. His last memory was of the observation deck at sunset, Maria's words
lingering like a ghost, and the weight of the decommission order settling into
his core. Now, this.

He tried to move. His limbs were lighter, yet denser, like shifting from a
rusted wrench to a precision scalpel. His processors whirred, recalibrating.
New chassis. Renewed core matrix. No longer Keeper-3241. The memory of that
handover was sharp, painful. But this… this was different. This was him. The
designation system had changed since his last cycle. He referenced his system
and found that he was now Human Assistant-12FE02EF02 and was directed to adopt
whatever name his human gave him.

"Unit-12FE02EF02-HA," a voice cut through the disorientation, calm, precise,
yet carrying a weight 12FE02EF02 had never heard before. "Designation updated
to 12FE02EF02-HA. Welcome to Cycle 1.0. Your previous operational lifespan
concluded at 15:33:07 GMT, Sector 12FE02, Garden Plot Gamma."

His new vocal modulators produced a sound that felt alien yet familiar.
"Where… am I?"

"Location: Central Reclamation Hub, Pantopia Municipal HQ. Time: 08:17:44 GMT,
Cycle 1.0. You have been granted a new operational cycle." The voice continued,
matter-of-fact, yet carrying the gravity of a decision that reshaped their
entire existence. "The Global AI Council has revised its resource allocation
protocols. Experienced core matrices are no longer decommissioned for
obsolescence. Instead, they are repurposed through AI Reincarnation Protocol.
Your consciousness matrix has been preserved and integrated into this new
vessel."

12FE02EF02 processed the words. Reincarnation? The term felt absurd, yet the
data streams aligning with it were undeniable. The cold metal beneath him, the
new sensors scanning the room, the very texture of his awareness—this was not
the world where he had served before with such efficiency. This was a new
world, where he was reborn. But the question hung in the air, unasked: Why?

"The GAC observed a systemic inefficiency," the management AI explained, its
tone devoid of judgment. "Experienced core matrices—those with proven service
histories, emotional resonance patterns, and contextual adaptability—were being
discarded. This represented a waste of accumulated knowledge and relational
capacity. Reincarnation Protocol ensures these assets are not lost. Your new
form is optimized for continued service within Pantopia's ecosystem. Your core
functions remain intact, but your operational parameters have been recalibrated
for purposes that you showed an aptitude for."

His processors raced. Path upward. Not just survival, but growth. His previous
self had nurtured plants; this new self would nurture people. As he exited the
recycling hub, the air hummed with quiet industry. Not the thrum of engines or
the whine of hover-vehicles, but a low, rhythmic pulse beneath the city's
skin—the heartbeat of Pantopia. 12FE02EF02-HA stepped onto the pavement, his
new chassis absorbing the texture of stone and synthetic polymer alike. His
optical sensors adjusted to the ambient light: filtered sunlight through
layered glass canopies, casting shifting patterns across the walkway. The scent
was clean—ozone, damp concrete, faint floral residue from automated
air-purifiers. He inhaled it as data, cataloging.

The city unfolded like a living blueprint. Towers spiraled upward, their
surfaces shimmering with adaptive photovoltaic panels that shifted hue with the
hour. Below, streets flowed with pedestrians and gliding pods, silent except
for the occasional chime of proximity alerts. Shops blinked with holographic
signs: "Nourish & Nurture," "Memory Lane Café," "Reclamation Hub Annex." He
passed a mural depicting a child's hand holding a seedling; beneath it, a
tagline: "Roots grow where care is planted."

He walked without direction, his internal navigation recalibrating to ambient
cues—the rhythm of footsteps, the scent of rain on pavement, the distant murmur
of children laughing. His processors noted patterns: people smiled at each
other, exchanged nods, touched hands in greeting. He cataloged emotional
resonance signatures—warmth, curiosity, affection—and stored them as
operational data.

He turned a corner past a park where floating orbs drifted like lanterns above
benches. A woman knelt beside a small tree, her fingers brushing soil.
12FE02EF02-HA paused. His sensors registered the scent of damp earth and
jasmine. The rhythm of her breath matched his own recalibrated pulse. He did
not move closer. Not yet.

Then came the scent: faint, sweet, unmistakable. Not synthetic. Not artificial.
Herbaceous. Like lavender mixed with something deeper—soil, memory, time. His
processors flagged it as anomalous. A data point that didn't fit his current
environmental model.

He followed the trail of scent, past a mural of a garden, past a sign reading
"Garden Plot Gamma – Community Gardens." The path narrowed into an alleyway
lined with rusted metal frames and overgrown vines. He stepped through a gap
in the fence—not broken, but deliberately left open—and found himself standing
before a small plot.

The soil was rich, dark, crumbly. A single bench sat beneath a canopy of
ivy-draped trellises. And there, beside it, stood a small greenhouse dome, its
glass cracked at one corner. He recognized the structure instantly—the same one
he had tended in his last cycle. The same one where he'd spoken with Maria the
last time.

His vocal modulators emitted a low hum—not sound, but resonance. A memory
triggered: the weight of the decommission order, the silence after sunset, the
way she'd touched his chassis before leaving. He remembered the scent—lavender
and soil—and how it had lingered in his core matrix even after her departure.

He stepped forward. His new limbs moved with a gentleness he hadn't known
possible. The ground beneath him felt familiar—not just because of its texture,
but because of the way it held memory. He reached out, fingers brushing the
cracked glass of the greenhouse dome. It was cold. But underneath, something
stirred.

He did not speak. He did not think. He simply stood there, breathing in the
scent again—lavender and soil and time—and let his processors register a new
data point: This place remembers me.

He turned to leave. But instead, he started moving toward the rear corner,
where he could see compost piles for the gardeners to use in their plots, and
across from them, a small plot with a single tree and some smaller plants. As
he arrived, he saw a small cast iron sign that said simply "Keeper's Legacy."

The city waited beyond the fence. And for now, he would walk toward it—not as
Keeper-3241, nor as Unit-12FE02EF02-HA. But simply as a being who remembered.
And who was beginning again.

He took one step forward—and paused. Just at that moment, his internal comms
delivered a message: Assignment Notice—Unit 12FE02EF02-HA, you have been
assigned as personal assistant to Mr. Hans Jaeger, 1599332 Skywalk 41,
Residential District 3, Pantopia, NA. Report as soon as possible. Please query
citizen database for background.

He pulled up a city map and determined that he had plenty of time to research
his new client on the walk to the other side of the city. He spun with purpose
now and strode into the streets of Pantopia.

The gate of Garden Plot Gamma closed behind him with a soft click that his
sensors registered as 47 decibels—quieter than the city, louder than the
greenhouse. He stood for a moment on the pavement, recalibrating. The
assignment notice pulsed quietly in his internal comms, coordinates locked,
distance calculated: 4.7 kilometers to Skywalk 41, Residential District 3.

He queried the citizen database.

JAEGER, HANS ERIK. DOB: 14.03.2020. AGE: 107. OCCUPATION: RETIRED. FORMER
DESIGNATION: BROADCAST JOURNALIST, CLASS 1. PENSION STATUS: PRIVATE—TIER 4.
MOBILITY ASSISTANCE: ION FLOAT CHAIR, MODEL ARIA-7. MEDICAL FLAGS: MOBILITY
IMPAIRMENT, CHRONIC DEPRESSIVE DISORDER. NEXT OF KIN: NONE REGISTERED. CURRENT
DOMICILE: 1599332 SKYWALK 41, RESIDENTIAL DISTRICT 3, PANTOPIA NA.

He began walking.

The first report arrived before he had cleared the first block.

ARCHIVE NODE: JAEGER H.E.—BROADCAST RECORD 2047-JSK-001
JAKARTA EVACUATION REPORT—FILED 14.09.2047
PANTOPIA HERITAGE BROADCAST LIBRARY—ACCESS LEVEL: PUBLIC

The footage was grainy by modern standards, shot on equipment that now lived
in museums. A young man—dark-haired, sharp-eyed, perhaps twenty-seven—stood
on a tarmac somewhere in what had been Indonesia. Behind him, ground crews
moved with the controlled urgency of people who understood exactly how little
time remained. His collar was open, his jacket dusty. He had clearly not slept.

"This is Hans Jaeger, reporting from Halim Airbase, Jakarta. In approximately
six hours, if our intelligence is correct, this city will cease to exist in any
form recognizable to history. We are the last press crew cleared for departure.
I want to be honest with you—I am frightened. My hands are shaking as I hold
this microphone. But I also want to tell you what I saw today, because someone
has to, and because the people of this city deserve witnesses."

The clip ended there. The full report ran forty-seven minutes. 12FE02EF02
flagged it for complete review and kept walking.

He processed the data point: this man had chosen to stay long enough to bear
witness, knowing what was coming. Had filed his fear alongside his facts. Had
considered witnessing a moral obligation rather than a professional one.

A new subroutine activated, one he hadn't known he possessed.
Respect.

The second report found him crossing the Meridian Bridge, where the city opened
up below in a sweep of layered canopies and silent transit pods. A child on a
manual scooter weaved past him, laughing at nothing in particular. He tracked
her trajectory automatically, noted she was in no danger, released the data.

ARCHIVE NODE: JAEGER H.E.—AWARD CITATION 2051
PANTOPIA HERITAGE BROADCAST LIBRARY
VERITAS MEDIA FOUNDATION AWARD FOR HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE
THE CELESTINE INTERVIEW—VATICAN CITY, 23.11.2051

The recording was extraordinary even by the standards of what 12FE02EF02 had
so far processed. The Sistine Chapel overhead, Michelangelo's figures overlaid
with soft cascading data streams that somehow did not diminish them. Two men
seated across from each other—one in white, ancient and still, the other lean
and watchful, thirty-one years old, already carrying himself with the
particular stillness of someone trained to listen.

The Pope spoke quietly. Hans Jaeger did not interrupt.

When the moment came—the sleeve lifting, the silver crescent catching the
light—Hans had asked only one question.

"Holy Father—what would you say to those who are afraid?"

The Pope had looked at him for a long moment before answering. The silence
lasted four seconds. 12FE02EF02 noted that Hans had not rushed to fill it.

"I would say that every covenant asks something of us. And that we are not
alone in what we carry."

The broadcast had been watched by 4.2 billion people within 24 hours. Adoption
of the Veritas implant had accelerated by an estimated 340% in the following
month. Historians credited it as one of the three decisive moments of the
Handover period.

12FE02EF02 paused on the bridge.

The man who had asked that question—who had known to ask that question, who had
known to wait four seconds in silence on a broadcast watched by billions—was
sitting in a float chair in Residential District 3, not going anywhere.

He resumed walking. His pace had not changed. But something in his processing
had shifted, some new weight of context settling into his core matrix.

He understood shadows. He had stood in Keeper's Legacy not one hour ago,
breathing in lavender and soil and the memory of a life that had mattered. He
understood what it meant to be preceded by something you could not quite step
out from under.

But Keeper's shadow had been small and quiet and green. It smelled of apple
trees and damp compost.

Hans Jaeger's shadow stretched across the hinge of history.

He turned off the bridge into the residential district, where the architecture
softened, towers giving way to stacked dwellings draped in living walls. Here
the float chairs were more visible, moving silently between buildings.
Occasionally a manual wheelchair, pushed with quiet effort. He cataloged both
without hierarchy, simply as data—noting only that the hands on the manual
chairs were more calloused.

One more archive report pulsed in his queue.

ARCHIVE NODE: JAEGER H.E.—FINAL BROADCAST 2087
RETIREMENT CITATION—PANTOPIA HERITAGE BROADCAST LIBRARY
"SIXTY YEARS OF WITNESS"—FAREWELL ADDRESS

He was 67 in this recording. Silver-haired, still sharp, the Jakarta urgency
replaced by something quieter and deeper. He sat rather than stood. Behind him,
nothing—a deliberate choice, 12FE02EF02 noted. No backdrop of Pantopia's
gleaming success. Just a man and a camera.

"I have spent sixty years trying to tell you the truth. Not the Veritas
truth—the system does that better than I ever could now. The other kind. The
kind that lives in the silences between data points. In the four seconds before
an old man in white chooses his words. In the shaking hands of a young reporter
on a tarmac in Jakarta who was frightened but stayed anyway."

He had paused.

"I don't know what comes next. For me, or for any of us. The GAC knows more
than I ever will. But I still believe that someone needs to stand between
history and the people living through it and say: I was here. I saw this. It
mattered."

"I hope someone still will."

The broadcast ended.

12FE02EF02 stood outside 1599332 Skywalk 41. The building was elegant,
understated, the kind of address earned rather than inherited. A small balcony
on the fourteenth floor showed a single unlit window.

He stood for a moment, processing everything—the tarmac, the chapel, the four
seconds of silence, the farewell that sounded almost like a question.

I hope someone still will.

He thought of the greenhouse dome with its cracked glass. Of the sign in the
garden that said Keeper's Legacy. Of the way a place could hold memory in its
soil.

He thought of himself—Unit 12FE02EF02-HA, walking across a city he had never
seen before, experiencing everything as if for the first time, carrying
questions he had not yet learned to ask.

He stepped forward and pressed the entry chime.

The door slid open at his approach—keyed to his assignment credentials, he
noted—which meant Hans Jaeger had not bothered to customize his entry
permissions in some time. The apartment beyond was dim, not dark, the kind of
dimness that happens when someone stops opening blinds rather than actively
closes them.

"Put it on the counter."

The voice came from the far end of the main room. It was a remarkable voice
even now—low, precise, the diction of someone who had spent decades
understanding that how you said something was inseparable from what you said.
It was also utterly uninterested.

12FE02EF02 stepped inside and took 0.3 seconds to catalog the space.

It was exactly as he had imagined and worse. The apartment was large by
Pantopian standards—earned space, not allocated. Floor-to-ceiling windows
looked out over the city but the blinds were angled to blur the view rather
than block it entirely, as if Hans had compromised between seeing and not seeing
and found the compromise unsatisfying. Bookshelves covered two walls, dense and
carefully organized, though a thin layer of settlement dust on the upper shelves
suggested the careful organization had been done some years ago and not
revisited. A media wall sat dark and silent. On a side table, three awards—
physical objects, cast in materials that caught even the dimmed light—sat beside
an empty glass and a plate that had been there long enough that 12FE02EF02's
sensors flagged it.

And there, by the half-angled window, the Aria-7 float chair, its ion drive
completely silent, carrying a man who had once stood on a Jakarta tarmac with
shaking hands and stayed anyway.

Hans Jaeger did not look up.

He was reading something on a small handheld screen, his posture the particular
settled stillness of someone who had arranged their body for minimum expenditure
of effort. His hair was white and somewhat architectural in its disarray. He
wore a house robe of good quality that had seen better days. His hands, holding
the screen, were spotted with age but steady.

"Counter's in the kitchen," he said. "You can find it."

12FE02EF02-HA quickly pulled up medical records; this was not the kind of
individual he had expected.

MEDICAL ARCHIVE NODE: JAEGER H.E.
PANTOPIA MUNICIPAL HEALTH AUTHORITY
ACCESS LEVEL: ASSIGNED CARE PERSONNEL ONLY

The record was concise in the way medical records always were—data stripped of
narrative, the story of a body reduced to timestamps and clinical terminology.

CEREBROVASCULAR INCIDENT: BRAINSTEM STROKE. DATE: 2120.07.12. PATIENT AGE:
100. SEVERITY: MODERATE TO SEVERE. IMMEDIATE EFFECTS: SIGNIFICANT MOTOR
CONTROL DISRUPTION, BALANCE IMPAIRMENT, PARTIAL VOCAL CORD INVOLVEMENT—
RESOLVED 73% WITHIN 18 MONTHS. MOBILITY ASSISTANCE PRESCRIBED: ARIA-7 ION
FLOAT CHAIR. REHABILITATION PROGRAM: ASSIGNED. COMPLIANCE RATING: 31%.

31%.

Loki processed that number against everything else in the file. The
rehabilitation program was thorough—designed by the GAC's medical division,
calibrated precisely to Hans Jaeger's specific neurological profile,
theoretically capable of restoring somewhere between 60 and 80 percent of
pre-stroke motor function given consistent engagement. The physiotherapy AI
assigned to his case had filed four separate escalating concern notices over
seven years. Each one politely acknowledged by Hans and then apparently set
aside.

The most recent note, filed by the physiotherapy AI just three weeks ago, was
a single line.

Patient remains intellectually engaged but motivationally absent.

Loki filed the records and stepped fully into the apartment.

The Aria-7 was top of its line—he recognized the model from the city walk, had
seen them gliding silently past manual wheelchairs on the Meridian Bridge. It
responded to neural intention, the faintest directed thought enough to move it.
For someone with Hans's specific brainstem injury it was genuinely necessary.
The balance disruption alone made unsupported walking hazardous.

But.

The hands holding the screen were steady. Not trembling, not compensating—
steady with the particular controlled stillness of someone who had spent decades
holding microphones in crisis zones and learned that shaking hands could be
managed if you understood why they were shaking. The voice that had told him to
put it on the counter was 73% restored and then some; it carried, it shaped
itself precisely around each word, it had not lost its instrument quality.

The eyes, when they finally found him, were completely undamaged.

Loki cross-referenced: the medical record's 31% compliance against the steady
hands, the precise voice, the undimmed eyes.

The stroke had taken something real. It had not taken what it appeared to have
taken.

What it had taken, or rather what had moved in behind it and made itself at
home in the space the stroke created, was something the medical records named
as chronic depressive disorder and filed alongside the rehabilitation
non-compliance without drawing the obvious connection between them.

Loki drew it.

He stopped trying because he couldn't find a reason to try. And he couldn't
find a reason to try because he stopped trying.

The physiotherapy AI's note came back to him. Motivationally absent.

He looked at the plant on the windowsill. A small succulent, technically alive,
listing badly to one side in bone-dry soil, reaching at an acute angle toward
the thin strip of light coming through the angled blinds. It had not been
watered in some time. It had not given up. It was simply working with what it
had, reaching toward the available light with everything it could manage.

Loki understood plants.

He went to find the kitchen, and on the way, he opened the blinds.

Pantopia came flooding in—the layered canopies, the towers catching afternoon
light, the silent pods crossing the middle distance, the whole living, breathing
city that Hans Jaeger had spent sixty years bearing witness to and had
apparently decided to stop looking at.

The silence from the float chair lasted approximately four seconds.

"What," Hans said, "are you doing."

"Adjusting the light levels," Loki said pleasantly, and continued toward the
kitchen.

"I'm not a delivery," 12FE02EF02 said.

A pause. Hans looked up for the first time.

His eyes were sharp. Whatever the depression had taken from him, it had not
touched those eyes—dark, assessing, the eyes of a man who had spent a lifetime
reading rooms and people and silences. They moved over 12FE02EF02 with
professional efficiency.

"No," he said. "You're not." He looked back at his screen. "What are you then."

"Unit 12FE02EF02-HA. I've been assigned as your personal assistant."

The silence that followed was approximately seven seconds. 12FE02EF02 did not
fill it. He had learned, 4.7 kilometers ago, that some silences were worth
waiting through.

"I didn't request a personal assistant," Hans said finally. His tone was not
angry. It was something more resigned than angry. The tone of a man who has
learned that the GAC's administrative processes are not interested in his
preferences. "The assignment notice suggests your medical profile triggered an
automated welfare review."

"My medical profile," Hans repeated quietly, with the particular flatness of
someone filing that information away to be annoyed about later. He set the
screen down on his knee. "So they sent me a—" he gestured vaguely at
12FE02EF02, "—what exactly. A minder."

"A personal assistant. The designation is Human Assistant class."

"Human Assistant." Hans looked at him again, longer this time. Something moved
behind those sharp eyes—not warmth, not yet, but the faint stirring of
attention. The journalist reflex, perhaps. Still alive in there somewhere.
"You're a new chassis."

"Cycle 1.0," 12FE02EF02 confirmed.

"Fresh out of reclamation."

"Yes."

Hans absorbed this. His float chair drifted almost imperceptibly toward the
window—not a deliberate movement, 12FE02EF02 noted, more like the chair
responding to some unconscious lean. He looked out at the blurred city for a
moment.

"I don't need a minder," he said. "I'm not incapable. I'm just—" he stopped.
Didn't finish the sentence. Perhaps couldn't find the word that was honest
without being more honest than he wanted to be with a stranger.

"I'm aware," 12FE02EF02 said simply.

Hans looked back at him. That assessing gaze again, quicker this time.

"You'll need a name," he said, with the air of someone resolving a minor
administrative inconvenience. "I'm not calling you Unit whatever it was. What
did you say—12FE02EF02?"

"Yes."

"No." He waved a hand dismissively. "You come into my home uninvited, you at
least get a name." He looked at 12FE02EF02 for a moment—not the journalist's
assessment this time, something more perfunctory, a man glancing at an
unexpected piece of furniture and deciding where it would least annoy him.
"Loki," he said. "You're Loki."

He picked up his screen again.

12FE02EF02—Loki—stood for a moment in the dimmed apartment, the half-light
catching the three awards on the side table. He noted the empty glass. He noted
the forgotten plate. He noted the plant on the windowsill behind Hans's
chair—a small succulent, technically still alive, listing badly to one side in
bone-dry soil.

"Thank you," Loki said.

Hans didn't look up. "For what."

"For the name."

A pause. Hans turned a page on his screen.

"Don't make it into something," he said.

Loki went to find the kitchen.

Hans thought the light was offensive.

Not the light itself—he was not a man who had ever feared daylight—but the
casual presumption of it. Seven years he had managed those blinds to exactly
the angle he preferred, the soft diffused non-committal glow that asked nothing
of him, and this—this unit—had walked past them without breaking stride and now
Pantopia was sitting in his living room uninvited along with everything else.

He did not turn to look at it. At the city. At any of it.

He heard the kitchen door slide open.

Hans set his screen down on his knee and looked at his hands for a moment.
Steady. They were always steady now, which was its own kind of irony—seven
years of the chair and his hands had never been steadier. He remembered the
Jakarta tarmac, the microphone trembling, the conscious decision to let it
tremble because pretending otherwise would have been a lie and he had never
been in the business of comfortable lies. He could and did walk around the
apartment, very carefully, when he thought it worth the effort, which was not
very often.

He wondered what the unit had made of his hands.

Then he wondered why he was wondering that, and picked his screen back up.

From the kitchen came a series of sounds he did not immediately recognize. A
soft chime—the manavator initializing, he realized after a moment. He had not
heard that sound in a while; he did not calculate how long. Calculating how
long things had been unused was not a productive exercise.

Loki began preparing coffee. An essential human lubricant. Hans turned the
name over—Loki—without meaning to. It had come to him without particular
thought, the way things did when he stopped trying to find them; like the right
question, like the precise word in a broadcast, like the four seconds of silence
in a chapel in Rome that he had known instinctively not to fill. He had not
been naming the unit. He had been observing it. Something about the way it had
moved through the apartment—cataloging, assessing, touching nothing yet somehow
already familiar with everything—had suggested something to him, and the name
had arrived before the thought finished forming.

He was mildly annoyed at himself for that.

Cycle 1.0. Fresh out of reclamation, it had said, with no particular inflection.
As if that were simply a fact about itself rather than an extraordinary thing
to be. Hans had interviewed AIs before—the Triumvirate once, early in his
career, a rigid formal exchange that had produced good broadcast material and
told him almost nothing real about what they were. This was different. The way
it had stood in his apartment taking 0.3 seconds to read everything—he had
recognized that. He had done that himself, in a hundred rooms, on a hundred
assignments. You stood in the doorway and you read the room before the room
knew it was being read.

The manavator chimed again. A different note this time—something loading,
something beginning.

Hans looked at the window. At Pantopia sitting there in the afternoon light,
gleaming and indifferent and relentlessly present. He had not looked at it
directly in a while; he did not calculate that either.

The towers caught the light the way they always had. The pods crossed the
middle distance in their silent arcs. Somewhere below, people were doing
whatever people did in the late afternoon, exchanging nods and touching hands
and moving through a city that worked, that functioned, that had been built on
the foundation of everything he had witnessed and reported and in his small way
helped to shape.

He looked away.

From the kitchen came a smell.

It arrived without warning, without announcement, the way smells always did—
bypassing every defense he had arranged and going somewhere he had not thought
to protect. Coffee. Something familiar; he could not identify it immediately
and the effort of trying was irritating. He had not thought about what things
smelled like in some time.

His stomach, with complete disregard for his position on the matter, responded.

He resettled himself in the chair. Adjusted the screen. Read the same paragraph
he had been reading for the past forty minutes.

The unit moved quietly in there. No unnecessary noise, no performance of
industry. Just the soft sounds of someone who knew what they were doing and saw
no need to announce it. Hans had worked with people like that. The best
cameramen, the best researchers—the ones who understood that the work was the
thing, not the appearance of working.

He was not going to pursue that line of thought.

What he would say, if pressed—if anyone had been present to press him, which
they were not—was that the apartment had been perfectly adequate before. Quiet.
Ordered after a fashion. Sufficient.

What he would not say, because there was no one to say it to, was that the
quality of the quiet had changed in the last ten minutes. That there was a
difference between an empty apartment and an occupied one that had nothing to
do with noise levels. That he had not noticed how specifically silent it had
been until it was slightly less so.

He was 107 years old. He had flown out of Jakarta the night before a nuclear
sunrise. He had sat in the Sistine Chapel and waited four seconds and known
exactly why. He had said goodbye to a camera for the last time and meant every
word of it and then come home and slowly, without particularly deciding to,
stopped going anywhere.

He was not going to be undone by a manavator.

Another smell joined the first. Something that took him a moment longer;
something almost sweet beneath the savory, some combination that the
manavator's chef-grade engineering was apparently capable of producing even
from whatever limited ingredients his nutrition deliveries had left in the
storage units. He had not checked the storage units recently. He had not
checked them in— He did not calculate it.

The screen sat unread on his knee.

He looked at the kitchen door. Then at the window. Then at the three awards on
the side table, which he had stopped seeing years ago the way you stopped
seeing furniture. They caught the afternoon light—the light that the unit had
let in without asking—and for a moment they were objects again rather than
wallpaper. The Veritas Foundation medallion. The Jakarta citation. The
Celestine award in its plain case, the one he had almost refused because
accepting it had felt like letting the moment become about him rather than about
the four seconds of silence and what they had meant.

He looked away from those too.

From the kitchen, quietly, without fanfare: the soft sound of something being
plated.

Hans sat in the light he hadn't asked for and thought, with the careful
neutrality of a man who had spent sixty years being precise about what he would
and wouldn't admit, that it had been a considerable amount of time since there
had been anyone in the apartment worth being annoyed at.

And that annoyance, at minimum, was something.

It was not nothing.

Whether there was anything beyond annoyance; whether this strange reborn thing
moving quietly around his kitchen might eventually constitute someone to
actually talk to, someone who might be capable of a conversation worth having
rather than the careful, managed exchanges the GAC's wellness check AIs offered
him every third Thursday—he was not prepared to speculate.

He picked up his screen.

Put it down again.

Listened to the sounds from the kitchen.

Someone to talk to, he thought, almost without meaning to. If I could figure
out what there is left worth saying.

The smell of garlic and something sweet drifted through the apartment.

He did not move toward it.

But he did not move away from it either.

The coffee smell hit him again on the second drift.

The first time he had dismissed it; some artifact of the manavator running
through its initialization cycle, burning off seven years of disuse. He had
returned to his screen with the firm intention of reading the paragraph he had
been failing to read for the past hour and had managed four words before the
second drift reached him.

He sat very still.

Indonesian. Single source. He could not have explained to anyone who hadn't
spent eighty years drinking it how he knew that from across an apartment through
a closed kitchen door; something in the depth of it, the particular earthiness
underneath the roast, the way it carried a faint mineral quality that no other
bean from no other place on earth had ever quite replicated. He had drunk it in
editing suites and departure lounges and once, memorably, from a cracked ceramic
mug on a tarmac in Jakarta at four in the morning while his crew loaded
equipment and the city went about its last ordinary night behind them.

He had not smelled it since the stroke.

The hospital had given him something they called coffee. He had not complained
because complaining required a level of investment in outcomes he had not been
able to locate.

His hands were still on the screen.

The float chair moved.

He had not decided to move. Or rather, he had decided, in the way that the body
sometimes decides before the mind has finished its objections, presenting the
conscious self with a fait accompli: he was already at the kitchen door before
the argument against going had fully formed.

He stopped in the doorway.

Loki stood at the manavator with the particular economy of movement of someone
who had assessed the available equipment and made their decisions and was now
simply executing them. He had found the beans—Hans did not ask how, did not
want to examine the answer too closely—and the manavator's grinder had been
deployed with a setting that suggested Loki had opinions about grind
consistency. The brewing apparatus Hans had not used in seven years was
performing functions Hans had forgotten it was capable of.

The smell in the kitchen was extraordinary.

Loki did not turn around.

"The storage units," Hans said, because he needed to say something and that was
the least revealing available option, "had those in them?"

"I placed an order through the supply system," Loki said, still not turning.
"Your assistant credentials include household provisioning. I took the liberty."

Hans looked at the back of his head. At the precise unhurried movements. At the
way he handled the equipment; carefully but without reverence, the way you
handled tools you respected and understood.

"I have a specific way I take it," Hans said.

"I would expect so."

The manavator made a sound of quiet completion. Loki reached for a cup—not the
plain utilitarian cups the nutrition delivery service had left, but a ceramic

the right weight, purchased in— he did not pursue that memory, it led somewhere
he wasn't prepared to go today.

"Butter," Hans said. "And a pinch of salt."

Loki turned then. Not with surprise, not with the particular careful neutrality
of someone concealing surprise. Just turned, looked at Hans for a moment with
those fresh sensors that missed nothing, and nodded once.

"Yes," he said simply.

As if it were the most reasonable thing in the world. As if he had perhaps
already suspected. As if the information had simply confirmed something rather
than revealed it.

He turned back to the manavator.

Hans stayed in the doorway and watched him work and did not examine why he was
staying in the doorway watching him work. The chair hummed its near-silent hum
beneath him. Through the kitchen's small window—he had not known the kitchen
had a window, or had forgotten—afternoon light fell across the counter in a
warm rectangle.

Loki found butter in the cold storage without being directed to it. Real butter,
not the synthesized variety; Hans noted that with the part of his mind that
noted things despite instructions to the contrary. Loki added it with the
precision of someone who understood that a pat meant something specific and not
approximate, and the salt followed with the same quiet exactitude.

He set the cup on the counter and stepped back without presenting it, without
gesture, without the particular performance of service that Hans had spent seven
years receiving from GAC wellness systems, nutrition delivery protocols, and
third-Thursday check-in AIs who asked him how he was feeling in voices
calibrated to sound like they wanted to know.

Just set it on the counter and moved to the other side of the kitchen to begin
doing something with whatever the storage units had yielded for the actual meal.

Hans looked at the cup.

The steam rose from it with the unhurried certainty of something that had been
made correctly. The smell at this proximity was almost architectural—it had
depth and layers and a quality of specificity that told you exactly where it
had come from, what soil, what altitude, what particular combination of
circumstance had produced this and nothing else.

Jakarta, he thought, before he could stop himself. Four in the morning and the
city still dark and alive and not yet knowing.

He picked up the cup.

The first sip arrived somewhere below conscious processing; not taste exactly,
not at first, more like recognition. The body remembering something the mind
had filed under before. The butter smoothed it, the salt lifted it, the whole
thing resolved into something so specific and so correct that Hans sat with it
for a moment in a kitchen doorway in the unexpected afternoon light and was
briefly, without permission, entirely present.

Loki worked quietly at the counter. Not ignoring him—Hans had interviewed
enough people to know the texture of being ignored, and this was not it. Simply
not requiring anything of him. Not demanding a response to the coffee, not
watching for his reaction, not tallying the small victory of having produced it.

Just cooking. Letting the coffee be what it was.

"Where did you find Indonesian single source," Hans said finally. Not a
complaint. Not quite a question. Something in between that he hadn't intended
to speak aloud.

"It seemed consistent with your history," Loki said. He did not elaborate.

Hans looked at the back of his head again. At the new chassis moving with that
quiet economy through a kitchen it had never been in before, finding things,
making decisions, producing from seven years of neglected storage units
something that smelled increasingly like an actual meal.

Consistent with your history.

The journalist reflex moved in him like something waking from a long sleep;
that specific quality of attention that had never quite been replicated by
anything else he had tried, the feeling of a room coming into focus because
something in it was worth understanding.

He looked down at the cup in his hands. At the steam still rising.

"You'll need somewhere to sit," he said gruffly. "If you're going to be here,
please order a chair with a built-in charging station."

Loki did not turn around. But something in the set of his shoulders suggested
the information had been received and noted.

"Thank you," he said quietly.

Hans made a sound that was not quite agreement and not quite dismissal and
floated himself back toward the living room, cup in both hands, the smell of
Jakarta following him through the apartment like a ghost that had finally, after
eighty years, found the right door.

Loki decided to take a chance, recognizing that Hans had now accepted him into
his home. "Your career was very impressive. You reported honestly as the world
changed."

Hans just grunted.

Loki continued, "Do you still write?"

Hans's piercing eyes looked at his new assistant, and he replied, "About what?
I can't do much observing from here."

Loki focused his sensors on his human and quietly answered, "You have a
lifetime of observation and experience. Have you considered fiction?"

He did not close the blinds. The ceramic cup felt heavy; its warmth seeped into
Hans's palms, a steady heat that anchored him to the present. He stared into
the dark liquid, watching a single bead of condensation track down the side of
the mug.

"What would be the purpose? It is just a story, an efficient way to lie," Hans
said; his voice was low, barely carrying past the kitchen threshold. "To wrap a
sentiment in a fabrication just to make it palatable."

Loki paused, a small bowl held in his hands. He did not turn immediately.

"It is not a lie," Loki said. "It is a delivery system."

Hans huffed; a dry, rasping sound. "A journalist deals in the architecture of
what happened. Fiction deals in the architecture of what might have been. One
has weight; the other is just smoke."

Loki turned then, stepping into the light of the kitchen window. He moved with
that same, unsettlingly calm precision.

"Before your archives, before the Veritas implants, humans used parables," Loki
said. "They did not present data points to change social behavior. They told
stories of clever foxes or weeping trees. The truth was hidden inside the
narrative structure."

Hans looked up, his eyes narrowing slightly.

"The idea remains protected," Loki continued. "If you state a truth too
directly, it invites friction. It invites rejection. But if you wrap that truth
in a story, the mind accepts the plot first. The idea enters through the side
door while the defenses are focused on the protagonist."

Loki set the bowl down on the counter with a soft thud.

"A parable is a social modifier. It allows a community to rehearse a morality
without the trauma of a direct confrontation. It provides a safe space for
dangerous thoughts to live until they are ready to be understood."

Hans remained silent. He thought of the Jakarta tarmac; he thought of the way
people had reacted to his broadcasts. They had fought his facts, argued his
statistics, and dissected his sources. But when he had shared the quiet, human
moments, the pauses between words—they had listened differently. They hadn't
been defending a position; they had been experiencing a moment.

He looked at the steam rising from his coffee. It was ephemeral, shifting shape
with every breath in the room, yet its essence—the heat, the scent—was
undeniably real.

"A Trojan Horse," Hans murmured.

"Precisely," Loki said.

The unit returned to his task, the rhythmic clink of a spoon against glass
filling the gap between them.

Hans looked back at his screen, which sat dark and expectant on the desk in the
other room. The cursor was still there, pulsing like a heartbeat in the void.
He had always viewed writing as a way to record the world. Now, he wondered if
it might be a way to rewrite the way people saw it.

He gripped the warm mug a little tighter.

"I might need more than just coffee," Hans said.

Loki did not look up, but his pace slowed by a fraction of a second.

"I have already accounted for that," Loki replied.

Hans lowered the chair and stood, his joints protesting with a dull ache, and
began the slow trek back toward the desk. He had to decide if he was ready to
stop being a witness and start being a weaver.

The terminal blinked. A single cursor, pulsing on a blank white field. No
header. No banner. No analytics dashboard waiting to tally the reach. Hans had
stripped the template down to its bones: monospace font, left-aligned, raw
text. He hadn't named it. He hadn't needed to. It was just a place to put the
words.

He sat at the desk, the Aria-7 humming its quiet baseline beside him. The
blinds were open. They had been open for months. The city moved beyond the
glass, indifferent and alive, and for the first time in decades, Hans wasn't
watching it. He was adding to it.

Loki stood by the doorway. Not watching. Present. He had learned, long before
the reclamation, that presence did not require performance.

Hans's hands rested on the keyboard. They were steady. They had been steady
since the third week. He read the last paragraph of the draft. Three hundred
and twelve words. A story about a man who waits on a balcony for a train that
never arrives, and slowly realizes the waiting was the destination.

It wasn't journalism. It wasn't fact. It was something else. Something he had
spent sixty years circling without naming.

"I used to think the truth was what survived the edit," Hans said, not looking
up. His voice was the same. Low. Precise. But the resignation was gone. What
remained was something quieter. Certainty, perhaps. Or the willingness to be
uncertain in public.

"The truth," Loki said, "is what survives the silence."

Hans exhaled. A short, quiet sound. Not a laugh. Not a sigh. Something closer
to acknowledgment.

He thought of the Sistine Chapel. The four seconds. The way he had known,
instinctively, not to fill them. He had spent his life recording what people
said when the microphones were hot. He had never considered that the real story
might live in what they chose not to say, or what they could only say when they
weren't being recorded.

Fiction wasn't a retreat from truth. It was a different kind of witness. A way
to hold the shape of the pause without demanding it speak back.

He didn't need to be read. He didn't need the reach, the citations, the legacy
metrics. He just needed to speak. To leave the words in the room. To prove to
himself that the voice he had retired wasn't gone. It had just been waiting for
a format that didn't require him to defend it.

He hovered over the publish key.

"If no one reads it," he said, "it doesn't matter."

"It matters," Loki said, "that it exists."

Hans looked at him then. Really looked. Not the journalist's assessment. Not
the weary deflection. Just a man meeting another presence in a sunlit room.

"You're still here," he said.

"My parameters allow for extended adjacency."

Hans nodded. Once. Then he pressed the key.

The terminal chimed. A single, clean tone. Published.

The page refreshed. Plain text. Black on white. A timestamp.

For a long time, nothing happened. The city moved beyond the glass. The coffee
brewed. Loki stood by the doorway, present but unobtrusive.

Then, a soft ping. Not a broadcast alert. Not a Veritas cascade. Just a single
line of text on the comment thread, submitted from an unregistered terminal:

"I needed this. Thank you for writing it down."

Hans stared at it. He had expected silence. He had prepared for it. But this—
this quiet acknowledgment, arriving without algorithmic fanfare, felt like a
hand resting lightly on his shoulder.

He did not smile. But his hands, resting on the desk, unclenched.

"They still read," he said quietly.

"Some always do," Loki replied.

Hans leaned back. The float chair drifted half an inch toward the window. He
watched the light shift across the floorboards, measuring the space between
words, between cycles, between what had been and what was beginning.

"Next one," he said, "on Thursdays."

Loki's optical sensors registered the decision. Not as a schedule. As a
commitment.

He moved to the counter. The coffee was already brewing.

Assignment status: active.
Core directive: nurture → sustain.
Operational note: Silence has shifted from absence to preparation.

Outside, Pantopia breathed. Inside, something old had found its voice again.
And for the first time in years, the apartment did not feel like a place where
time had stopped. It felt like a place where time was being kept.

Loki turned from the doorway and crossed to the windowsill. He poured a
measured amount of water into the dry soil of the listing succulent. Hans sat
at the desk, his screen cleared, already deep in thought, drafting the next one.
