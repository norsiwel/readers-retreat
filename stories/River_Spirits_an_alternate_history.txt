River Spirits
by Norsiwel

Chapter 1: Whispers on the Wind

Awinita paused at the threshold of her home, her fingers lingering on the smooth, living wood of the doorframe. The structure responded to her touch with a subtle warmth—a greeting, a recognition. Like all dwellings in Okwaho, the city breathed with its inhabitants, a symbiotic relationship cultivated through generations of careful tending and deep respect. The morning sunlight filtered through the translucent amber leaves that formed her roof, casting a honeyed glow across the polished clay floor. Outside, the city was already humming with activity.  "You're going to be late again," came her mother's voice from the central hearth. Makawee stood surrounded by clay pots of various medicinal herbs, her hands working methodically to grind a mixture of willow bark and river mint. "The Mound Keepers don't appreciate tardiness, especially not from an apprentice."  Awinita sighed, slinging her woven satchel across her shoulder. "The Pulse feels strange today," she said, ignoring her mother's gentle admonishment. "Can't you sense it? There's something... hesitant about it."  Makawee's hands stilled for a moment before resuming their rhythm. "It's the changing of the seasons. The transition always brings fluctuations." Her voice was steady, but Awinita caught the flicker of concern in her eyes. "Now go. Nahimana has much to teach you."  Outside, Okwaho unfurled before her in concentric circles of living architecture. Unlike the rigid, imposed structures of Britannia Nova to the east, or the cold, sterile crystalline towers of the Western Alliance, the city of Okwaho grew from the earth itself. Homes and communal buildings sprouted organically from carefully cultivated trees, their trunks and branches coaxed over decades into the desired forms. Walls of interwoven saplings created spaces both private and connected to the greater whole. Pathways of crushed stone wound between buildings, following the natural contours of the land rather than forcing straight lines upon it.  Awinita navigated these familiar paths, nodding greetings to neighbors and friends as she made her way toward the center of the city. Women in brightly patterned cotton dresses carried baskets of fresh harvests—squash, corn, and beans grown in the spiral gardens that surrounded the city. Men wearing intricately beaded vests over simple cotton shirts transported materials for craft and construction—clay from the riverbanks, reeds for weaving, and carefully harvested wood for carving. Children darted between adults, their laughter a counterpoint to the deeper rhythms of the city. But beneath the familiar bustle, Awinita sensed a subtle disharmony. The trees that formed the living infrastructure of Okwaho usually sang with vitality, their life force a constant, reassuring hum beneath the consciousness of those attuned to such things. Today, that song faltered occasionally, like a musician missing notes in an otherwise flawless performance.  As she approached the city's center, Okwaho's most distinctive feature came into view: the Great Mound. Unlike the smaller, residential mounds scattered throughout the Tamanend Empire, the Great Mound of Okwaho rose nearly sixty feet above the surrounding landscape, its perfect earthen dome crowned with a temple of living wood and stone. Elegant ramps spiraled around its circumference, allowing access to the various levels of the structure. At its summit, the Council of Elders gathered daily to discuss matters of governance and to commune with Tecumseh's Pulse—the spiritual energy that flowed through the land, guiding and sustaining the Tamanendi people.  At the base of the mound, a figure in deep blue robes awaited her. Nahimana, the senior Mound Keeper of Okwaho, stood with her eyes closed, one palm pressed against the earthen slope of the mound. Her silver-streaked hair was braided elaborately with turquoise and copper beads, marking her high status among the spiritual leaders of the city.  "You're late," Nahimana said without opening her eyes. "But perhaps it is fortuitous. The Pulse speaks strangely today, and I would value your unbiased perception before I share my own."  Awinita felt a flush of pride at her mentor's words. Despite Nahimana's strict demeanor, the older woman had always respected Awinita's unusual sensitivity to the Pulse. It was this sensitivity that had marked her for apprenticeship as a Mound Keeper at the unusually young age of sixteen, rather than the traditional twenty summers.  "I noticed it on waking," Awinita admitted, setting her satchel down and kneeling beside her mentor. "It feels... hesitant, somehow. Like a river encountering an obstacle and searching for a new path."  Nahimana nodded, her eyes still closed. "Place your hands on the earth and tell me what else you perceive."  Awinita did as instructed, pressing her palms against the cool, packed soil of the mound. Immediately, the sensation she had been peripherally aware of all morning intensified. The Pulse of Tecumseh—named for the legendary prophet who had united the Native nations three centuries earlier—was the lifeblood of their civilization. It flowed through the network of mounds that dotted the landscape of the Tamanend Empire, connecting communities in a web of spiritual and practical exchange. Through the Pulse, news traveled faster than any messenger could carry it. Through the Pulse, the harvests were coordinated, the weather patterns predicted, and the harmony of the land maintained.  But today, that steady rhythm faltered. Beneath her sensitive fingertips, Awinita felt the usual warm current of energy, but it seemed to withdraw momentarily before returning, like the tide pulling back from the shore.  "There's a... reluctance," she said, struggling to find the right words. "As if the Pulse is trying to avoid something. I feel it most strongly from the western tributaries."  Nahimana finally opened her eyes, their dark depths troubled. "You perceive clearly, Apprentice. For three days now, I have felt this disruption growing stronger. Yesterday, I communed with Keeper Takoda in Miskogee to the west. They report a similar disturbance, more pronounced than what we feel here."  The older woman rose fluidly to her feet, brushing soil from her robes. "Come. Today's lessons will be practical rather than theoretical. The Council must be informed, and we must prepare for a Deepening Ceremony."  Awinita felt a jolt of surprise and apprehension. The Deepening Ceremony was rarely performed—a ritual that allowed Mound Keepers to sink their consciousness into the Pulse itself, traveling along its currents to distant locations in spirit form. It was dangerous work, requiring immense concentration and spiritual fortitude. Apprentices were normally only allowed to observe, not participate.  "Am I ready for such a ceremony?" she asked, unable to keep the uncertainty from her voice.  Nahimana's expression softened slightly. "No one is ever truly ready for their first Deepening. But your sensitivity to the Pulse is exceptional, and these are exceptional times. I would rather have you beside me, partially prepared, than rely solely on my own perception."  They ascended the spiral ramp together, passing smaller shrines and meditation alcoves carved into the mound. Around them, the city of Okwaho continued its daily rhythms. From this height, Awinita could see beyond the central districts to the agricultural zones that surrounded the city—concentric rings of cultivation that transitioned gradually from the densely planted inner gardens to the managed forests at the periphery.  The Tamanendi agricultural system was a marvel of sustainability, lauded even by the technology-obsessed scientists of the Western Alliance. Rather than imposing monoculture rows upon the land, as was common in Britannia Nova, the Tamanendi people cultivated integrated ecosystems. Staple crops of corn, beans, and squash grew together in spiral patterns, the beans fixing nitrogen in the soil while climbing the corn stalks, the broad leaves of squash plants providing ground cover to retain moisture and prevent weeds. Fruit and nut trees were interspersed throughout, providing additional harvests, shade, and habitat for beneficial insects and birds.  Beyond these cultivated zones lay the managed forests—woodlands carefully tended to maximize diversity and resilience while providing a sustainable harvest of timber, medicinals, and wild foods. Finally, at the outermost rings, the true wilderness began, respected and largely untouched except for ceremonial purposes and carefully limited hunting.  But today, even from this distance, Awinita could see subtle signs of the disharmony she felt in the Pulse. The corn plants stood tall, their tassels golden in the morning light, but their leaves seemed to droop slightly despite the ideal growing conditions. In the nearest section of managed forest, a stand of maple trees displayed patches of yellowing leaves—months too early for their autumn transformation.  "Do you see?" Nahimana asked softly, following her gaze. "The land speaks its distress in many ways, for those with eyes to see."  "Is it drought?" Awinita asked, though the recent rainfall had been plentiful.  "No, not drought. Something more insidious. The Pulse carries nutrients and energy throughout our lands, just as blood carries life through our bodies. What we feel is like a... constriction of those pathways. A blockage forming somewhere upstream."  They reached the summit of the Great Mound, where the Temple of Communion stood. Unlike the more organic structures of the residential areas, the Temple combined living materials with precise geometric design. Its eight walls were formed by ancient oak trees, their trunks grown deliberately to create perfect columns. Between these living pillars, panels of tightly woven reeds created walls that could be rolled up or down depending on the needs of the ceremony. The roof was a marvel of engineering—a series of concentric wooden rings suspended from the central point, supporting a canopy of treated hide that allowed a diffuse, golden light to permeate the interior.  Inside, several members of the Council of Elders were already gathered. Chief Councilor Huritt, his broad shoulders draped in a mantle of woven turkey feathers and copper plates, stood at the center of the eight-sided chamber. Around him, representatives from each of the city's major guilds formed a circle—the Master Cultivator with her crown of dried corn husks; the Master Artisan wearing his ceremonial mask of carved cedar; the Master Healer clutching his staff wrapped with the shed skin of a timber rattlesnake; and others, each marked by the symbols of their expertise and authority.  "Keeper Nahimana," Chief Huritt acknowledged with a respectful inclination of his head. "We were about to send for you. Disturbing reports have arrived from the western settlements."  "The Pulse has already spoken of disruption," Nahimana replied, stepping into the circle. Awinita remained at the edge of the gathering, as was appropriate for an apprentice. "What news comes by more conventional means?"  The Master Cultivator, an older woman named Kanti who had revolutionized the three-sisters planting techniques decades earlier, stepped forward. "Crop failures in Miskogee and Tanasi. Not complete, but significant. Plants wither despite adequate rain and tending. More troubling, the living architecture shows signs of stress—homes weakening, branches becoming brittle."  "Similar reports from Echota," added the Master Healer, his weathered face grave. "And a new illness among the children there—nothing immediately life-threatening, but unusual. A lethargy, a paleness, as if their very vitality is being slowly drained."  Awinita felt a chill at these words. The Tamanendi people rarely suffered from the waves of disease that periodically swept through the coal-smog cities of Britannia Nova. Their understanding of medicinal plants, clean water systems, and balanced nutrition kept them generally healthier than their neighbors to the east. An unexplained illness was cause for serious concern.  "We must consult the Deep Pulse," Nahimana said firmly. "I propose a Deepening Ceremony, with my apprentice in attendance." She gestured toward Awinita, who felt the weight of every elder's gaze suddenly upon her.  Chief Huritt studied Awinita with piercing black eyes. "Awinita, daughter of Makawee. You have shown remarkable attunement to the Pulse, but a Deepening is not to be undertaken lightly. Are you prepared to accept this responsibility and risk?"  Awinita wanted to look to Nahimana for reassurance but knew this moment demanded she stand on her own strength. She straightened her shoulders and met the Chief Councilor's gaze directly.  "I am prepared, Chief Huritt. I have felt the disturbance in the Pulse growing stronger each day. If my sensitivity can be of service to Okwaho and the Tamanend people, I offer it willingly."  A murmur of approval rippled through the gathered elders. Even the usually stern Master Artisan gave a slight nod of acknowledgment.  "Very well," Chief Huritt decided. "The Deepening Ceremony will commence at moonrise. Keeper Nahimana, make the necessary preparations. The Council will gather representatives from each district to observe and bear witness."  As the Council members dispersed to their various responsibilities, Nahimana led Awinita to a small meditation chamber built into the side of the Great Mound. Here, far from the bustle of the city, the walls were lined with clay vessels containing sacred plants—sweet grass, tobacco, cedar, and sage—the four plants used in purification ceremonies throughout the Tamanend Empire.  "The Deepening requires absolute clarity of mind and spirit," Nahimana explained, selecting several dried leaves from each vessel. "We must fast until the ceremony, and purify ourselves with these sacred medicines."  She crushed the leaves between her palms, releasing their aromatic oils, then passed her hands through the smoke of a small clay brazier burning in the center of the chamber. The mingled scents of the sacred plants filled the small space, sharpening Awinita's senses even as they calmed her mind.  "What exactly happens during a Deepening?" Awinita asked, mimicking her mentor's purification actions. She had witnessed standard communion with the Pulse—the daily rituals that kept the Mound Keepers connected to the energy flow—but the Deepening was spoken of only in general terms during her training.  Nahimana was silent for a long moment, arranging ceremonial items on a woven mat—a copper bowl filled with spring water, a clay pipe inlaid with riverbed pearls, a fan made from the wing feathers of a red-tailed hawk.  "During a Deepening," she finally said, "we do not merely touch the surface of the Pulse, as we do in daily communion. We surrender ourselves to it entirely, allowing our consciousness to flow with it to distant places. Our physical bodies remain here, but our spirits travel the currents of Tecumseh's Pulse throughout the land."  She looked up, fixing Awinita with an intense gaze. "It is disorienting. Overwhelming. Some apprentices describe it as drowning, others as flying. The sensations of your body will fall away. You will see through eyes that are not eyes, hear through ears that are not ears. And you must maintain your sense of self throughout, or risk becoming lost in the flow."  A shiver ran through Awinita, equal parts excitement and apprehension. "Has anyone ever... not returned?"  "Yes." Nahimana's answer was blunt. "Three times in my lifetime, Keepers have been lost to the Deep Pulse. Their bodies lived on for a time—days, sometimes weeks—but their spirits never returned. They withered and died, empty shells without the spark of consciousness."  Seeing the shock on Awinita's face, Nahimana's expression softened slightly. "This is why we train for years before attempting a Deepening. But you have advantages many  apprentices lack. Your connection to the Pulse is unusually strong, and your mind has a natural discipline that some never achieve despite decades of practice."  She placed a reassuring hand on Awinita's shoulder. "I will be with you every moment. If I sense you becoming lost, I will pull you back. Trust in your training, incomplete though it may be."  The rest of the day passed in meditation and preparation. Awinita struggled with the fasting requirement—not because of physical hunger, but because the absence of food left her mind free to conjure increasingly elaborate scenarios of what might go wrong during the ceremony. By the time the sun began to sink toward the western horizon, bathing Okwaho in golden light, her nerves were strung as tight as the strings of a cedar-wood harp.  The city took on a different character as evening approached. The living architecture of Okwaho responded to the changing light—bioluminescent fungi cultivated within certain wood species began to glow with a soft blue-green light, illuminating pathways and public spaces. Families gathered for evening meals, the scents of corn cakes, roasted venison, and herb-rich stews wafting through the air, making Awinita's empty stomach clench in protest.  When Nahimana finally came to escort her back to the Temple of Communion, the full moon was just beginning to rise, a perfect silver disk climbing above the eastern horizon. The city below had grown quieter, though not silent. The Deepening Ceremony was significant enough that many citizens had chosen to hold vigil rather than retire to their homes, gathering in small groups around communal hearths to await news.  The Temple at the summit of the Great Mound had been transformed. The reed walls had been rolled up, opening the space to the night air. Eight ceremonial fires burned in copper braziers placed at each of the eight cardinal and intermediate directions. Between them, representatives from each district of Okwaho sat in respectful silence, forming an outer circle around the central space where the Council members stood.  At the very center of the Temple, a depression in the floor had been filled with water from the sacred spring that bubbled up within the Great Mound itself. The surface of this shallow pool reflected the moon and stars above, creating the illusion of a cosmic gateway.  Chief Huritt stepped forward as Awinita and Nahimana entered. He wore full ceremonial regalia now—a headdress of eagle feathers trailing down his back, a breastplate of hammered copper and freshwater pearls, leggings decorated with intricate porcupine quillwork.  "The moon has risen," he announced, his deep voice carrying easily through the hushed Temple. "The time of Deepening is upon us. Let all who witness remember what they see and hear this night, that the wisdom gained may benefit all our people."  Nahimana led Awinita to the edge of the central pool. Together, they disrobed down to simple white cotton shifts, then accepted ceremonial mantles made of softened deer hide painted with sacred symbols—Nahimana's emblazoned with the Tree of Life, Awinita's with the spiraling pattern that represented the journey of the soul.  The ritual began with purification. Four young women—daughters of Council members, chosen for their purity of spirit—approached from the four cardinal directions, each carrying one of the sacred plants. They circled Nahimana and Awinita, wafting the smoke of sweet grass, tobacco, cedar, and sage over them with eagle feather fans, while chanting prayers in the ancient language of the Tamanendi people.  Next came the offerings. Nahimana and Awinita each took a pinch of sacred tobacco and cast it into the nearest brazier, watching as the smoke curled upward toward the star-filled sky.  "We offer this sacred plant," Nahimana intoned, "that our prayers may rise to the Creator and the ancestors. We seek not power for ourselves, but understanding for our people. Guide us through the currents of the Pulse, that we may identify the source of disharmony and work to restore balance."  Finally, they stepped into the central pool. The water came only to their ankles, but its significance went far deeper—this was water from the very heart of the Great Mound, charged with the concentrated energy of the Pulse. As it touched Awinita's skin, she felt an immediate tingling sensation, as if the water itself were alive with microscopic currents of electricity.  Nahimana seated herself in the center of the pool, the water soaking the hem of her shift and ceremonial mantle. She gestured for Awinita to sit facing her, their knees almost touching.  "Take my hands," she instructed, extending her palms upward.  Awinita placed her hands in her mentor's. Nahimana's skin felt unnaturally warm, almost feverish, and Awinita could feel a subtle vibration passing between them.  "Close your eyes," Nahimana said, her voice already taking on a dreamy, distant quality. "Feel the Pulse beneath you, around you, within you. Do not reach for it—allow it to rise and enfold you."  Awinita closed her eyes and focused on her breathing, using the meditation techniques she had practiced for years. She became aware of the Great Mound beneath her—not just as an earthen structure, but as a living entity pulsing with energy. The Pulse was always present at the edge of her consciousness, but now she allowed herself to sink into it, becoming fully aware of its rhythms and currents.  At first, it felt like floating in a warm river. Images flickered behind her closed eyelids—fragments of landscape, glimpses of other mounds throughout the Tamanend Empire, flashes of ceremonies taking place at distant locations. Then, gradually, the sensation deepened. The boundaries of her body seemed to dissolve, her consciousness expanding outward along the channels of the Pulse.  She was vaguely aware of Nahimana's voice guiding her, but it seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once. "Don't fight the current. Let it carry you. Maintain your center, but allow yourself to flow."  Suddenly, Awinita was no longer in the Temple of Communion. She was rushing along invisible pathways, seeing landscapes blur beneath her as if flying at impossible speed. She caught glimpses of other Great Mounds in distant cities—Tanasi with its famous triple mounds arranged in a perfect equilateral triangle; Miskogee with its mound shaped like a bird in flight; Echota with its network of smaller mounds connected by raised earthen pathways.  "Focus your intention," came Nahimana's voice, somehow still connected to her despite the vast distances they were traversing. "Seek the source of the disruption."  Awinita concentrated on the feeling of wrongness she had sensed earlier—the hesitation in the Pulse, the sense of something blocking its natural flow. Immediately, her spiritual perception shifted, pulling her westward with increasing speed. The landscape below changed from the familiar rich forests and meadows of the central Tamanend territories to more arid regions.  Disjointed images flashed through her awareness: a dried-up creek bed where water should be flowing; a stand of cottonwood trees with withered leaves; a small farming community where the crops stood stunted in the fields; a circle of elders gathered around a child lying unnaturally still on a bed of woven reeds.  Then, abruptly, she encountered resistance. It felt like swimming against a powerful current, or trying to walk into a strong wind. The farther west she tried to push her perception, the stronger this resistance became.  "I can't go further," she called out, hoping Nahimana could still hear her. "Something is blocking the way."  "I feel it too," came the response, Nahimana's presence suddenly closer in this strange, non-physical space. "This is not natural. The Pulse is being deliberately diverted or dammed."  With a shared intention that required no words, they pushed against the invisible barrier together. For a moment, it seemed to yield slightly, and Awinita caught a fragmentary glimpse of something beyond—a vast structure of metal and glass, incongruous in the natural landscape. Steam or smoke rose from multiple chimney-like protrusions. The land around it was barren, devoid of the life force that flowed through the rest of the territory.
Then, without warning, a surge of energy pushed back against them—not the natural flow of the Pulse, but something artificial, something wrong. Awinita felt a sharp pain lance through her consciousness, and heard Nahimana cry out in alarm.  "Back!" Nahimana commanded. "Return now! Follow my voice!"  Awinita felt herself being pulled rapidly backward, retracing the spiritual path they had traveled. The landscapes blurred beneath them once more, but now in reverse. She caught one last glimpse of that strange structure before it vanished from her perception, replaced by the more familiar territories of the central Tamanend lands.  With a gasp, Awinita opened her eyes. She was back in her physical body, sitting in the shallow pool at the center of the Temple of Communion. Her shift and mantle were soaked with sweat despite the cool night air. Across from her, Nahimana looked equally shaken, her normally composed features drawn with exhaustion and concern.  Around them, the gathered witnesses sat in stunned silence. Awinita realized with a shock that many hours must have passed—the moon had traversed much of the night sky and now hung low in the west.  Chief Huritt stepped forward, offering a hand to help Nahimana rise from the pool. "What did you discover, Keeper?" he asked, his voice tense with barely concealed anxiety.  Nahimana accepted his assistance, her legs unsteady as she stepped onto dry ground. Another Council member brought forward a blanket to wrap around her shoulders, while the Master Healer did the same for Awinita.  "The disruption comes from the western borderlands," Nahimana said, her voice hoarse. "Near the boundaries with the Western Alliance. There is... an artificial blockage of the Pulse. Some kind of structure, technological in nature."  Murmurs of concern rippled through the assembled witnesses. Relations with the Western Alliance were generally cordial, if somewhat distant. The border between the two nations was largely unfortified, marked primarily by natural features rather than military installations. Trade occurred regularly, with the Alliance eagerly purchasing Tamanendi agricultural products, medicinals, and artisanal crafts in exchange for certain technological conveniences that did not violate Tamanendi principles of harmony with the land.  "Are you suggesting deliberate interference?" Chief Huritt asked, his expression grave.  Nahimana hesitated. "I cannot say with certainty. We encountered resistance when we attempted to observe more closely. But the structure we glimpsed was not natural, nor was it in keeping with the agreed-upon development protocols for the border regions."  The Master Artisan stepped forward, his ceremonial mask pushed up to reveal his concerned face. "Could it be one of their energy generation facilities? The Alliance has been expanding their fusion research."  "Perhaps," Nahimana conceded. "But never before has Alliance technology interfered with the Pulse. Whatever this is, it's different. Invasive."  "And it's making people sick," Awinita added, finding her voice at last. The gathered elders turned to her in surprise, not expecting the apprentice to speak in such a forum. But having experienced the Deepening, she felt compelled to share what she had witnessed. "I saw a settlement—I think it was White Clay Creek. There was a child there, ill with something the healers couldn't identify."  The Master Healer nodded grimly. "This matches the reports we've received. Children and elders affected first, then adults. Fatigue, weakness, difficulty concentrating. As if their connection to the Pulse is being drained away."  Chief Huritt was silent for a long moment, weighing the information. Finally, he straightened his shoulders, decision made.  "We must investigate further, but not through spiritual means alone. I will dispatch a delegation to the western territories immediately. Master Healer, select your most skilled diagnosticians to accompany them. Master Cultivator, we need specialists who can assess the impact on the land and crops."
He turned to Nahimana. "Keeper, once you have recovered your strength, I would ask you to join this delegation. Your direct experience of the disruption will be invaluable."  Nahimana inclined her head in acceptance. "I will go. And my apprentice should accompany me." She placed a hand on Awinita's shoulder. "Her perception during the Deepening was exceptional. She may sense things that even I cannot."  Awinita felt a mixture of pride and apprehension at these words. Travel to the border regions would mean leaving Okwaho for the first time in her life. Yet she could not deny the pull she felt—a responsibility to the land and people she had glimpsed during the Deepening.  "It is decided, then," Chief Huritt announced. "The delegation will depart at dawn the day after tomorrow. Let messengers be sent to inform the western settlements of our coming. And let us hope this disturbance can be addressed before more harm comes to our people and our land."  As the gathering began to disperse, Awinita remained seated, wrapped in her blanket, still processing the experience of the Deepening. The glimpse of that strange structure lingered in her mind—metal and glass where there should be wood and stone, straight lines where there should be curves, lifelessness where there should be vitality.  "What did we see?" she asked Nahimana quietly, as her mentor settled beside her. "What could the Western Alliance be building that would affect the Pulse so dramatically?"  Nahimana's eyes were troubled as she gazed westward, toward territories now hidden by distance and darkness. "I don't know, Awinita. In all my years as a Keeper, I have never felt the Pulse so... wounded. But whatever it is, we must convince them to stop before the damage spreads further."  "And if they refuse?" Awinita asked, voicing the concern that hung unspoken in the air.  Nahimana had no answer, and in her silence, Awinita heard the whisper of change on the wind—a premonition that the harmonious balance between the three nations of North America was about to be tested as never before.  Outside the Temple, the first hint of dawn touched the eastern horizon. A new day was beginning, but Awinita sensed it was more than that. A new chapter in the history of the Tamanend Empire was unfolding, and for better or worse, she would play a part in its writing.
Chapter 2: Visions in the Grove
Dawn light filtered through the ancient oaks of Kidahkinah Grove, transforming the morning mist into a golden veil that hung between earth and sky. Eliza Whispering Willow knelt at the center of the sacred space, her palms pressed against the cool earth, fingers spread wide as if to better receive the whispered secrets of the soil. Around her, seven white stones formed a perfect circle—one for each of the great spirits that guided the Six Nations Confederacy. Though the stones appeared ordinary to untrained eyes, Eliza could see the faint luminescence that pulsed within them, strongest at dawn and dusk when the veil between worlds was thinnest.  She had come to the grove before sunrise, slipping away from the sleeping settlement of Ganondagan while the nightwatch embers still glowed in the community hearths. It was not unusual for her to seek solitude here; as apprentice to Chief Shaman Soaring Eagle, such communion with the spirits was expected, encouraged even. But today's visit carried greater urgency than mere practice or reflection.  For three nights now, her dreams had been haunted by images she could not dismiss as ordinary nightmares. They clawed at her consciousness even in waking hours, flashing behind her eyes when she least expected: withered corn stalks bent like  supplicants before an unseen force; forest streams running dark with something that was neither mud nor blood; ancestral mounds weeping a strange, silver fluid that burned the earth where it fell.  "Show me," she whispered, her voice barely disturbing the reverent silence of the grove. "Show me what troubles the land."  Eliza reached into the pouch at her waist and withdrew a small handful of sacred herbs—sweet grass, sage, and tobacco mixed with dried petals from the vision flower that bloomed only during the full moon. She scattered them in a spiral pattern around her, then retrieved a clay vessel containing glowing embers carried from the eternal flame kept by the shamans of Ganondagan. With practiced movements, she set the herbs alight, watching as tendrils of fragrant smoke rose to dance with the morning mist.  As the smoke enveloped her, Eliza closed her eyes and began to sing—not the structured ceremonial songs taught in the shaman's lodge, but an intuitive melody that rose from somewhere deep within her, guided by the spirits themselves. The notes flowed like water, rising and falling in patterns that mirrored the land's contours surrounding the grove. Her voice, usually soft-spoken in the settlement, took on a resonant quality that seemed to emanate not just from her throat but from her entire being.  The world around her began to change. Though her eyes remained closed, Eliza could "see" the grove transforming. The boundaries between physical reality and the spirit realm blurred, colors intensifying beyond their natural hues. The trees stretched skyward, their forms elongating until they resembled ancient guardians with gnarled faces peering down at her from their bark. The earth beneath her palms seemed to pulse with a heartbeat distinct from her own—the rhythm of Tecumseh's Pulse, the lifeblood of their civilization.  "Eliza Whispering Willow," came a voice unlike any human sound—it was the rustle of leaves, the murmur of underground streams, the whisper of wind through grass, all woven into coherent speech. "You seek understanding."  "Yes," she replied, speaking aloud though she knew the communication transcended ordinary language. "The dreams come stronger each night. The land is trying to tell me something."  "Not dreams. Visions." The voice seemed to come from everywhere at once now—the stones, the trees, the very air she breathed. "Look."  The grove vanished, and Eliza found herself standing at the edge of a vast cornfield. Unlike the thriving agricultural lands that sustained Ganondagan, these plants stood sickly and stunted. Yellow leaves hung limply from bent stalks, and where ears of corn should have been forming, there were only withered nubs. The soil beneath appeared dull and lifeless, lacking the rich darkness that signified fertility.  As she watched, the scene shifted. Now she stood beside a forest stream she recognized—it ran just three miles west of Ganondagan, a reliable source of clear water and fat silver fish. But in her vision, the water ran thick and sluggish, with an oily sheen across its surface. A deer approached the opposite bank, lowered its muzzle to drink, then recoiled as if tasting something bitter.  "What is poisoning the water?" Eliza asked, but received no immediate answer.  The vision shifted again. Now she stood before one of the sacred mounds—not the Great Mound of Ganondagan, but one of the smaller ancestral mounds that dotted the territories of the Six Nations. Ordinarily, these mounds glowed with a subtle phosphorescence visible only to those with spiritual sight, a manifestation of the ancestors' continued presence and blessing. But this mound was different. Its surface appeared cracked, like parched soil in drought. From these cracks seeped a strange, silvery substance that looked like neither water nor blood but had qualities of both—reflective like polished metal yet flowing like a liquid.  Where this substance touched the ground, plants withered instantly. A butterfly that flew too close  dropped dead in mid-air. The mound itself seemed to pulse with pain, as if the very earth were wounded and festering.  "It spreads," the voice said, now tinged with something Eliza had never before heard from the nature spirits—fear. "From west to east. The Pulse weakens. The harmony breaks."  "What causes this?" Eliza demanded, fighting to maintain her composure as the vision grew increasingly disturbing. "Show me the source."  The scene shifted one final time. Eliza found herself hovering above the landscape, carried westward at impossible speed. Forests and meadows blurred beneath her, settlements appearing as brief constellations of earthen and wooden structures before vanishing behind her. The land gradually changed character—the lush eastern woodlands giving way to drier terrain, though still bearing the careful management of Tamanend stewardship.  Finally, at the very edge of Tamanend territory, where their lands bordered the Western Alliance, she saw it. A massive structure unlike anything built by her people—all gleaming metal and sharp angles, with great towers that reached skyward like accusatory fingers pointed at the heavens. Steam or smoke rose from several points, and surrounding the facility was a perimeter of barren earth where nothing grew.  Most disturbing of all was what Eliza's spiritual sight revealed—the Pulse, which should have flowed like an invisible river through this land as it did throughout Tamanend territory, seemed to be drawn inexorably toward this structure. But rather than passing through or around it, the energy was somehow being... absorbed. Captured. Redirected.  As she tried to look closer, to understand the purpose of this unnatural edifice, a searing pain lanced through her consciousness. It felt as if she had touched something electrically charged, a sensation no Tamanend citizen would ordinarily experience. The vision shattered like glass, fragmenting into disjointed images that assaulted her senses—children lying listless in their beds; elders unable to rise from their mats; plants withering in once-fertile fields; and most terrifying, the sacred mounds growing dark, their connection to the spiritual realm weakening.  Eliza gasped, her eyes flying open as she was thrust violently back into her physical body. She found herself still kneeling in Kidahkinah Grove, but now her clothing was soaked with sweat despite the cool morning air. The ceremonial circle of herbs had burned completely to ash, and the seven stones that surrounded her had lost their subtle glow, appearing now as ordinary river rocks. Standing at the edge of the grove was Soaring Eagle, the Chief Shaman of Ganondagan. His weathered face, lined by eight decades of communion with the spirits, showed neither surprise nor disapproval at finding his apprentice conducting an unsupervised vision quest. "I did not expect—"  "The spirits called me here, just as they called you," Soaring Eagle said, approaching with the measured steps of one who had long ago learned that haste was the enemy of wisdom. "I have witnessed your vision through their eyes."  "Then you saw it too? The structure at the western border? The sickness spreading from it?"  Soaring Eagle nodded gravely. "I have seen fragments in my own dreamwalking these past weeks. But never so clearly as through your sight." He reached out, placing gnarled fingers against Eliza's forehead. "You have been blessed—or perhaps cursed—with exceptional clarity of vision. The spirits have chosen you as their primary messenger in this matter."  Eliza felt a weight settle onto her shoulders at these words—not a physical burden, but the gravity of responsibility. Among the Six Nations Confederacy, those with true shamanic gifts were deeply respected but also understood to bear an obligation to use those gifts in service to the people and the land. This is not a matter for shamans alone—it threatens all our people, and perhaps those beyond our borders as well."  "Will they listen?" Although the Six Nations Confederacy honored its shamans, the governing Council was primarily pragmatic in its approach, focused on trade agreements, agricultural planning, and diplomatic relations with neighboring territories. To the western border. To see this thing with my physical eyes."  It wasn't a question, but Soaring Eagle nodded anyway. "Some truths can only be fully understood through direct experience. At twenty-two summers, she was considered an adult by the standards of her people, but her position as shaman's apprentice had kept her largely within the boundaries of their settlement and the surrounding sacred sites. Since childhood, she had listened to tales of the vast territories that comprised the Tamanend Empire—from the eastern shores where Britannia Nova began, to the western plains where the Six Nations Confederacy shared an uneasy border with the Western Alliance. It is right that this should pass to you now, when you embark on the most important task of your apprenticeship."  The significance of this gift was not lost on Eliza. Among the shamans of the Six Nations, such ceremonial tools were considered extensions of their wielder's spiritual authority. Unlike other settlements of the Six Nations, Ganondagan had been governed primarily by women for seven generations, ever since the legendary diplomat Jigonhsasee had established it as a center for peace negotiations between formerly hostile tribes. Running Deer's expression remained neutral, but Eliza could sense her skepticism. The Head Councilor was known for her practical approach to governance, preferring concrete evidence to spiritual insights. Despite her pragmatism, Running Deer was no fool—she understood the value of the shamanic tradition, even if she sometimes questioned its interpretations. With renewed purpose, she followed Soaring Eagle into the shaman's lodge to prepare for the journey that would take her from apprentice to something more—a true voice for the spirits in a time when the very foundations of their world trembled on the edge of profound change.  
 Chapter 3: Broken Arrow's Shadow

The wind carried the scent of ash and decay, a bitter tang that stung Charles Broken Arrow Petty’s nostrils as he crouched atop a ridge overlooking the Tsalagi River. Below him, the water flowed sluggish and dark, its surface marred by an oily sheen that caught the late afternoon sun in unnatural rainbows. Once, this river had run clear, its banks alive with fish and otters. Now, the otters were gone, the fish floated dead in fouled eddies, and the only sound was the groan of dying willows along the shore. Charles tightened his grip on the ashwood bow across his knees, fingers tracing the carvings of wolf and hawk—symbols of his clan, etched by his grandfather decades ago. The weapon was a comfort, but it felt inadequate today, a relic against a threat no arrow could pierce.  At forty-seven summers, Charles bore the weight of years spent patrolling the eastern edges of the Tamanend Empire in Cherokee territory. His frame was broad and hardened, silver threading his black braid, lines etched deep into his weathered face. His dark eyes, sharp as obsidian, scanned the horizon where lush forests gave way to scarred borderlands. Beyond the river, gray smoke rose from Britannia Nova’s coal-driven factories, their stacks jagged against the sky like a predator’s teeth.  He spat into the dirt, a gesture of habit and disgust. The eastern border had always been contested, a clash between Tamanend’s reverence for the land and the industrialized greed of its neighbors. But lately, the encroachment had worsened—black sludge choking streams, acrid fumes poisoning the air, metallic residues coating the soil. The Tsalagi River was one casualty among many, its death throes a warning Charles couldn’t ignore.  He signaled to the warriors waiting below. Three figures emerged from the trees—Tsali, a wiry youth with a scarred cheek; Kanuna, a broad-shouldered woman with steady hands; and Yonah, the eldest, his graying hair tied back. They wore Cherokee scout garb: deer hide tunics dyed earthy reds and browns, adorned with beadwork.  “Anything new, War Chief?” Kanuna asked, her voice rough. She nodded toward the river. “Looks worse than last moon.”  Charles nodded grimly. “Slower current. More dead fish. That smell’s stronger.” He pointed downstream. “We’ll follow it, see how far the poison’s spread.”  They fell into step behind him, navigating the riverbank where decay grew starker. A dead heron lay among reeds, feathers matted with slime. Shriveled blackberries crumbled to ash at Charles’s touch, leaving a metallic residue.   “Britannia Nova’s filth,” Yonah muttered, spitting. “They call this progress.”  “Not just filth,” Charles said. “It’s deliberate—concentrated near the border, thinning westward. They’re testing us.”  Tsali kicked at fouled mud. “Why don’t we burn their factories? Make them pay.”  “And start a war we can’t win?” Charles shot back. “Their machines outpace us. We’d burn a few buildings, and they’d raze our villages.”  Tsali’s jaw tightened, but he stayed silent. Charles felt the same anger—a fire banked in his chest. He’d seen Britannia Nova’s might: steel-clad soldiers, cannons, airships raining fire. Tamanend had survived by avoiding such conflicts, but those defenses were crumbling now, eroded by an enemy that didn’t need troops—just waste.  Further downstream, they found a doe and fawn sprawled dead, bodies twisted unnaturally. The doe’s eyes were clouded, foam crusted her mouth. A silver liquid trickled from her nostrils, pooling in the dirt—shimmering like quicksilver, humming faintly.  “What in the ancestors’ name…” Kanuna whispered, stepping back.  Charles crouched, avoiding the fluid. “Poison from their machines. It’s not just waste—it kills fast.”  Yonah peered at it. “This isn’t natural. Like a wound bleeding wrong.”  Charles nodded, his mind racing. The Mound Keepers spoke of the Pulse as the land’s blood—sustaining life. This was a festering wound, spreading corruption.  “Mark this,” he told Tsali. “Warn the villages—no drinking, no fishing.”  Tsali tied a red cloth to a branch. Charles’s gaze drifted to the smokestacks, close enough now to see the factories—brick and iron hulks spewing slag into glowing pits. Beyond lay Britannia Nova’s cities, a skyline of soot and steel. A new threat, tied to the east’s decay.  Charles stood, trembling. The silver fluid here mirrored the vision’s poison. Were they connected—two wounds bleeding into one? He’d guarded the east his whole life, but this called him beyond, to the blight’s heart.  He glanced at his bow, then the sleeping Wolves. Tradition urged him to stay, defend his post. But the vision demanded more—a journey, a reckoning. He wasn’t a Mound Keeper or spirit-walker, yet the land had chosen him, a war chief, to act.  At dawn, he’d tell them. Not just of machines, but this call. Kanuna could lead the patrol back to Ani-Yunwiya, warn the healers. He’d go west—alone if needed—drawn by a force he didn’t understand, to face a blight threatening all Tamanend.  The wind whispered through the oaks, carrying no answers, only urgency. Charles felt a shadow lengthen across his soul—change, inevitable as dawn. He’d walk into it, bow in hand, a warrior stepping beyond tradition into a battle he couldn’t yet name. 

 Chapter 4: Seeds of Heresy

Chapter 5: The Earth Trembles
Morning fog clung to the valleys of Chahta, one of Tamanend's most revered spiritual centers, where seven ancient mounds rose like sleeping giants from the misty plains. The settlement bustled with life as it had for centuries—a harmony of tradition and purpose. Market stalls lined the central square, their cedar frames adorned with strings of dried herbs and smoked fish. Children darted between traders, their laughter mingling with the steady rhythm of women pounding corn in wooden mortars. Near the riverbank, fishermen mended nets while singing work songs passed down through generations.  At the heart of it all stood the Great Mound of Chahta, towering thirty feet above the plain, its earthen slopes covered in vibrant wildflowers. Unlike the smaller mounds scattered throughout Tamanend territory, this one served not as a burial site but as a living conduit—a place where the Pulse flowed strong enough for even the untrained to feel its resonance. For eight generations, no ritual or council gathering began without first drawing strength from the mound's presence.  Atop this sacred hill stood Nimkii, the settlement's Lead Keeper, her gray-streaked hair tied with red and yellow ribbons that matched her ceremonial mantle. Her weathered hands traced patterns in the air as she led morning prayers, twenty disciples arranged in concentric circles around her. The ritual was as familiar as breathing—a daily affirmation of Tamanend's connection to the land.  "Feel the rhythm beneath your feet," she instructed, her voice carrying on the morning breeze. "The ancestors spoke through the soil long before we had words. Listen not with your ears, but with your—"  She stopped mid-sentence, her brow furrowing.  One of her younger apprentices, a boy named Kitchi, looked up curiously. "Keeper Nimkii? What's wrong?"  The old woman said nothing, dropping to her knees and pressing her palms flat against the earth. The disciples exchanged uncertain glances as she closed her eyes, her expression tightening with each passing moment.  "Something's not right," she finally whispered, her voice suddenly frail. "The Pulse... it stutters. Like a heart struggling to beat."  Before anyone could respond, a low rumble rose from beneath them—not the comforting vibration of the Pulse, but something discordant, like stone grinding against stone. Birds erupted from nearby trees, their panicked calls piercing the morning stillness. At the market below, conversations faltered as people paused, heads turning toward the mound.  Nimkii struggled to her feet, her eyes wide with alarm. "Everyone off the mound! Now!"  Her disciples scrambled to obey, some slipping on the dewy grass in their haste. In the square below, Oturia, a seasoned scout recently returned from the western territories, recognized the danger before most. "Everyone to the river clearing!"  Mothers scooped up children. Elders leaned on younger arms. The community moved with the practiced coordination of people accustomed to forest fires and storms—natural threats they understood. But this was different. This was sacred ground betraying them.  Atop the Great Mound, Nimkii had begun a desperate ritual, her hands digging into the soil as she chanted ancient words of binding and healing. Sweat beaded on her forehead despite the cool morning air. "Hold fast," she pleaded, speaking not to her people but to the earth itself. "Hold together. Remember your purpose." From the safety of the river clearing, the people of Chahta watched in horror as the unthinkable unfolded. The Great Mound—their anchor, their heart—was breaking apart. Nimkii remained at its peak, her diminutive figure silhouetted against the brightening sky, still performing her ritual even as the ground shifted beneath her.  "Someone get her down from there!" a voice cried from the crowd.  Chapter 6: An Unlikely Alliance

The dawn broke over Chahta with a sickly pallor, the sun struggling to pierce a haze of dust and smoke that lingered from the previous day’s catastrophe. The settlement, once a vibrant tapestry of living architecture and sacred mounds, lay in disarray. Eliza Whispering Willow moved among the wounded, her hands trembling slightly as she applied a poultice of sage and river mint to a young boy’s arm. She closed her eyes briefly, reaching for the Pulse—the spiritual energy that flowed through the land, connecting all life in Tamanend. Normally, it was a warm, steady current beneath her senses, but today it flickered erratically, like a flame caught in a gusting wind. The corruption she had glimpsed in her visions—the silver fluid, the dying streams, the wounded mounds—had come crashing into reality here at Chahta.

Dust coated his leather tunic, and a streak of soot marred his weathered face, but he showed no sign of fatigue. As he coordinated the rescue efforts, Charles caught sight of Eliza tending to the injured. Her focus was undeniable, her hands deft as she worked with herbs and whispered chants. He had heard of her—Soaring Eagle’s apprentice from Ganondagan, gifted with visions. They’re part of us.”  Charles rubbed his chin. “And I know you think I’m blind to the unseen. I don’t deny your visions—I’ve had one myself. With a final nod, Eliza stepped forward, leaving the familiar behind as she ventured deeper into the unknown, guided by the whispers of the spirits and the pull of destiny. The path was uncertain, their trust fragile, but the stakes left no room for hesitation.

Chapter 7: Whispers of Cahokia's Fall
Three days of hard travel had left Eliza and Charles weary but resolute. They crested a final ridge, and there below them sprawled the legendary Floating Markets of Cahokia—or what remained of them.  "Great Spirit," Eliza whispered, her hand tightening around her staff.  Charles stood beside her, his weathered face grim. "I visited here five summers ago. It was... different then."  The famed waterways of Cahokia had once been the veins of Tamanend's commerce, carrying goods and knowledge across the nations. Now, they languished beneath a thick carpet of silvery-green algae that pulsed with an unnatural rhythm. The central basin, once clear enough to count fish twenty feet below, had turned the color of tarnished copper. The smell reached them even at this distance—rot and stagnation, undercut by something metallic and wrong.  "The Pulse is barely moving here," Eliza said, reaching out with her senses. "It's like watching someone struggle to breathe."  Charles nodded, his skepticism temporarily set aside. Even without spiritual sensitivity, the wrongness of this place was palpable. "Come on. Stay close."  They descended toward the markets, following a path that wound between abandoned gardens. Once-cultivated plots now sprouted twisted vegetation—corn stalks bent at impossible angles, their husks gleaming with silver sheen; squash vines that coiled like serpents around decaying trellises.  The first traders they encountered were huddled beneath tattered awnings, their wares laid out on blankets rather than the traditional floating platforms. A woman with hollow eyes offered them dried fish that gleamed with an unnatural luster.  "Safe to eat," she insisted, her voice cracking. "Cleaned three times in spring water. No silver sickness here."  Charles shook his head politely. "We seek information, not food."  "Information costs more than fish these days," the woman replied, turning away.  They moved deeper into the market, where skeletal structures of old floating docks now stood half-submerged in the clouded water. A few boats still operated, poled by gaunt figures wearing protective gloves and masks of woven reeds. Children no longer played in the shallows; instead, they sat in clusters on the banks, their games subdued and their laughter rare.  Eliza approached a medicine seller, an elderly Ojibwe man whose booth displayed remedies in clay pots and tightly sealed gourds.  "We've come from Chahta," she said quietly. Then the crops. Now people sicken too." He gestured to a corner where several figures lay on mats, their skin marbled with metallic streaks. "Those who drink from certain streams or eat the wrong plants. The silver sickness comes for them all."  Charles stepped closer. "And toward the west? The Alliance border?"  The medicine seller's expression hardened. "Go three docks down. Ask for River Shadow. She trades in rumors... and other things."  They thanked him with a bundle of uncontaminated herbs from Eliza's pouch and continued through the market. The contrast between Cahokia's legendary reputation and its current state grew more stark with each step. Platforms that had once hosted elaborate ceremonies now sagged under meager displays of withered crops and trinkets fashioned from salvaged materials.  "This was the heart of our trade network," Charles murmured. "Goods from all five nations passed through here. Now look at it."  "More than goods," Eliza added. "Stories, medicine, knowledge." She paused, watching a group of elders huddled in conversation, their ceremonial robes faded and patched. "The spiritual center is failing too." "You trade with outsiders," he observed, careful to keep judgment from his voice.  River Shadow's smile was thin. "We all do now. The question is whether we admit it." "The council doesn't know how far this has spread."  "The council sits in their untouched eastern territories, clinging to traditions while we watch our way of life dissolve," River Shadow said. "But they'll learn soon enough. The silver sickness spreads eastward with each passing moon."  Charles returned the device to the chest. "We need to know more about these towers. Locations, defenses."  River Shadow considered them. "I can connect you with guides who know the western routes, for the right price." Her gaze fell on Eliza's pouch. They passed a once-sacred gathering place where shamans had communed with water spirits for generations. Now it stood abandoned, the water too contaminated for rituals.  As dusk settled over Cahokia, they found lodging with a river clan elder who offered them space in her home in exchange for some of Eliza's medicinal herbs. From the woman's elevated dwelling, they could see the entirety of the market as lanterns flickered to life, casting weak light across the diminished activity below.  "It used to shine like a field of stars," the elder told them, her voice thick with memory. "Boats coming and going all night, music, trading calls. The Pulse was so strong here you could feel it humming in your bones."  Eliza nodded, watching a solitary trader pole his boat through the algae-choked channel. "We'll find a way to restore it."  The elder's smile was sad. "Perhaps. But some say we should embrace the new way. My grandson left to work for the Alliance last spring. Says their machines are the future, that the Pulse was always going to fade."  Charles, who had been silent, turned to her. "And what do you believe?"  "I believe balance can be restored," she said after a moment. "But not by clinging solely to the past or rushing blindly toward the future. The middle path is hardest to walk."  Later, as Charles kept watch and Eliza tried to rest, she found herself unable to sleep. The wrongness in the Pulse vibrated through her awareness, a discordant note in what should have been harmony.  "Something troubles you beyond the obvious," Charles said, noticing her restlessness.  Eliza sat up. "River Shadow's brother said he saw our people working with the Alliance. If that's true…"  "It changes things," Charles finished her thought. "Tomorrow we get our guide. Then we find these towers and see the truth for ourselves."  Eliza nodded, though uncertainty gnawed at her. The corruption of the Pulse was devastating enough, but the thought of her own people turning away from their most sacred traditions shook her to her core.  Outside, a mournful song drifted up from the water—river clan children singing an ancient blessing for the waters, their young voices rising with determination despite the decay surrounding them. In that moment, Eliza found a spark of hope. As long as the songs continued, something essential remained alive.  Dawn would bring new challenges, new allies, and the next step toward confronting whatever force threatened their world. For now, she closed her eyes and let the children's voices carry her toward sleep, their song a defiant promise that Cahokia—and all of Tamanend—would not surrender without a fight.
Chapter 8: River Clan Secrets
The north dock stood apart from the main Cahokia markets, a weathered extension of wooden planks reaching into a secluded inlet. Unlike the bustling central waterways—or what remained of their bustle—this area maintained a deliberate quietude. Eliza and Charles arrived as the first light of dawn broke through a haze of silver-tinged mist, casting long shadows across the algae-choked water. "You came," she called, her voice carrying across the water. "And without bringing guardians or elders. Wise."  As the boat neared the dock, Eliza noticed how it cut through the contaminated water with minimal disturbance, leaving almost no wake behind. She whistled a complex pattern in response, then turned to them. "Change of plans. We need to move. Now."  With practiced efficiency, she unmoored the vessel and pushed off from the dock. "Looking for us?"  "Unclear. But they mentioned 'unauthorized technology transfer.'" The woman glanced at Eliza and Charles with unmasked suspicion. "Your new friends bring trouble." The mission was becoming more complicated by the moment, but they couldn't afford to lose a vital informant.  "What's your plan?" he asked. The people moving about the settlement wore a mixture of traditional Tamanend clothing and items clearly scavenged from beyond the borders.
