                                  Uncommon Sense
                                   by Norsiwel

The world, for twelve-year-old Diego Ávila, was a symphony of controlled chaos, and its grand conductor was his grandmother.

Her orchestra pit was the Tuesday feira livre on Rua Mourato Coelho, a roaring, steaming, vibrant artery carved through the Pinheiros neighborhood of São Paulo. The air, thick enough to taste, was a complex broth of competing aromas: the sweet, heavy perfume of overripe mangoes and jackfruit piled high on canvas tarps; the sharp, salty tang of frying acarajé, its dendê oil popping in massive woks; the mineral scent of wet pavement as a vendor sluiced down his stall; and underneath it all, the faint, ever-present perfume of diesel from the city’s groaning buses.

Diego’s place was at the right hand of his Vovó, Elena Ávila. She was a short, formidable woman whose strength seemed to emanate from the very ground she stood on. Her domain was a simple wooden stall laden with the building blocks of Brazilian life: sacks of black beans and farinha, pyramids of tomatoes and onions, and the crown jewels—gleaming, fat avocados from a cousin’s small farm in Minas Gerais.

Vovó Elena operated on a system older than any currency, a complex algorithm of the human heart. Her ledger was a dog-eared, grease-stained notebook she called O Caderno. Its pages were a frantic spiderweb of names, dates, and numbers—a testament to a community’s intricate dance of credit and debt, favors and trust.

“Observe, Diego,” she’d say, her voice a low rumble above the market’s din. She’d nod toward a young woman, Dona Silva, nervously clutching a few crumpled reais. “See her eyes? The worry for her children is there, plain as a crack in a plate. But her pride is there, too. It is a wall.”
Diego watched as his grandmother engaged. 

She didn’t offer a handout. She didn’t sigh with pity. She picked up an extra-large avocado, its skin a perfect, pebbled green. “For the little ones,” she said, her tone firm, as if it were a non-negotiable part of the transaction. “They need their strength.” 

She added the avocado to the woman’s bag and made a tiny, almost invisible mark in O Caderno. It wasn't a debt to be collected with interest, but a balancing of the social scales, a human transaction that no machine could ever parse.

Diego was her apprentice. He learned to weigh beans with a flick of the wrist, to spot the sweetest papaya by the blush on its skin, and to make change in the dizzying blur of hands and coins. But the real lessons were in the spaces between the numbers. He learned to read the slight hesitation in a customer’s voice, the shame in a downturned gaze, the relief that bloomed in a smile. Vovó’s system was inefficient, messy, and deeply, profoundly human. It ran on a currency of reputation and empathy.

“Seu Afonso,” Vovó would call to the baker two stalls down, holding up a bag of onions. “A trade for three of your best pães de queijo?”

Seu Afonso, a man with flour in his eyebrows, would laugh. “Only if you promise these onions won’t make my wife cry more than my jokes do, Elena!”

A deal was struck. No money changed hands. It was a thread in the vast, invisible tapestry of their community. Diego, at twelve, believed this tapestry was unbreakable. He believed his grandmother, a solid mountain of love and common sense, was the anchor holding it all together.
The breaking of the world began not with a bang, but with a whisper.

It started as a subtle change in the market’s frequency. The usual boisterous hum faltered, replaced by tight knots of conversation, hushed and urgent. Diego saw it first in the faces—the usual market-day expressions of haggling and greeting were curdled with a new, sour anxiety. The symphony was off-key.

Then, a man Diego knew—Senhor Costa, who ran the electronics repair shop—stumbled through the crowd, his face ashen. He wasn't shouting; he was making a low, wounded sound, like a dying animal. He collapsed against the side of a fruit stall, sending a cascade of oranges rolling across the grimy pavement. People gathered, their whispers turning into a panicked buzz.
“FuturoBrilhante,” someone breathed. The name passed from lip to lip like a contagion. “It’s gone. All of it.”

Diego’s blood went cold. FuturoBrilhante Investimentos. The Bright Future Investments. It had swept through the neighborhood over the last year, a promise whispered over fences and in cafes. A community-driven fund, they’d said. High-tech, AI-optimized, designed for the little guy. A way to fight back against the faceless global banks. It was a digital Ponzi scheme dressed in the language of empowerment, and it had been irresistible.

He turned to his grandmother. Vovó Elena’s hands, usually in constant motion—weighing, bagging, gesturing—were perfectly still on the wooden counter. Her knuckles were white. He had seen his grandmother angry, tired, and joyful. He had never, in his entire life, seen her look fragile.
Dona Silva, the same woman who had received the gift of the avocado, ran to their stall, tears streaming down her face, her voice a raw shriek. “Elena! It’s all gone! The money for the rent, for the school… everything!”

Vovó didn’t answer. She stared straight ahead, her gaze fixed on something far beyond the chaos of the market. Diego watched as the unshakeable mountain of his world began to crumble into sand. The invisible tapestry he’d so admired was unraveling, thread by painful thread. All the handshakes, all the trust, all the little marks in O Caderno meant nothing when the foundation they were all standing on had been a lie.

He looked down at the open notebook on the counter. The list of names, the small debts of trust and produce, now looked like a monument to naivete. It was a record of kindnesses she had extended, credit she had offered from her own pocket. A pocket that was now empty. The system of human empathy had been devoured by a system of calculated greed. His grandmother, the conductor of the market’s symphony, had been silenced.

The days that followed were suffocatingly quiet. The vibrant, noisy apartment shrank, its walls seeming to close in around the silence. Vovó Elena moved through the rooms like a ghost, her presence a hollow echo of the force she had once been. The smell of cooking was gone, replaced by the scent of dust and stale air. She would sit by the window for hours, not looking at the sprawling, energetic city below, but through it.

Diego did the chores, trying to fill the void with activity. He coaxed his younger sister, Sofia, to eat. Sofia, only eight, was a creature of pure light and optimism. She didn't fully understand the magnitude of what had happened, only that Vovó was sad and the apartment was too quiet.

One afternoon, as a light rain smudged the view from their window, the holovision flickered to life. It was a global news feed, and the image that coalesced in the middle of their small living room was a stark, jarring contrast to their reality. Gone was the grit and grime of São Paulo. In its place was a sterile, blue-and-white laboratory, gleaming under cool, efficient lighting. 

The chyron at the bottom of the screen read: OSLO, NORWAY: A NEW ERA OF TRUST?
A man appeared on screen. He was tall and severe, with a face that looked as if it were carved from Nordic granite and absolute certainty. His name was Erik Vinter.

A crisp, British voice narrated over the images of flowing code and pristine servers. “After his own grandmother’s pension was systematically drained by digital fraudsters, Norwegian cryptographer Erik Vinter vowed to create a system impervious to human greed.”

The report cut to Vinter himself. He spoke with no passion, only the cold, unassailable confidence of a mathematician presenting a proven theorem. “The flaw in our financial systems has always been the human element. Trust is a vulnerability. It can be exploited. We have engineered a solution where trust is no longer required. The code is the trust. The math is the guarantee.”

He called it SikkerKjede. The Secure Chain.

The narrator returned, her voice swelling with historic importance. “SikkerKjede is a new form of blockchain, an immutable, transparent, and incorruptible public ledger. Today, the governments of Norway, Sweden, and Denmark have formally adopted it as their primary financial backbone. 

Early reports indicate a staggering 98% reduction in all forms of financial fraud.”
The screen showed smiling, well-dressed Nordic citizens tapping their dataphones, their transactions completing with a satisfying, clean chime. It was a vision of a perfect world. A clean world. A world without risk, without fraud, without the messy, unpredictable variable of human fallibility.

Sofia, who had been listlessly drawing on a tablet, sat up, her eyes wide with wonder. She pointed a small finger at the glowing hologram.

“Vovó, listen!” she chirped, her voice piercing the room's heavy silence. “Look, they fixed it! They fixed the stealing! Vovó, that man… he could have saved you!”
The innocent cruelty of her words hung in the air like poison.

Diego’s gaze snapped from the sterile, confident face of Erik Vinter to his own grandmother. Vovó finally turned from the window. There was no anger in her eyes, only a profound, bottomless weariness. She looked at the hologram of Vinter, a man lauded as a global savior, and Diego saw not a flicker of hope in her expression. 

He felt a deep, seismic disconnect. Vinter’s solution, so elegant and logical on screen, felt like a profound insult here, in this room, in the presence of this specific, human pain. It was like offering a sterile, adhesive bandage for a gangrenous wound.

He turned to his grandmother, the question bubbling up from a place of confusion and burgeoning anger. “Is that right, Vovó? Can a code stop people from being greedy? Can it stop them from lying?”

Vovó Elena slowly pushed herself out of her chair. She walked over to Diego and cupped his face in her hand. Her palm was cool and dry. She looked into his eyes, and for the first time in days, he saw a glimmer of the old, wise conductor.

“Eles tiraram as pessoas do problema, meu neto,” she said, her voice a soft, raspy whisper. “But the problem is the people. The good and the bad. The trust and the greed. It is all one thing.” She stroked his cheek. “What happens when you try to erase that?”

The question settled deep in his bones, a seed of doubt planted in the ruins of his certainty. He looked over at Sofia. She was still mesmerized by the screen, her small face illuminated by the cool blue light of Vinter’s utopia. She saw a savior. Diego, looking at his grandmother, saw a cage. 

In that moment, he saw the future cleaving his own family in two: the bruised, heartbreaking skepticism of the old world, and the bright, blind optimism of the new.
He felt something in his pocket. It was a nutrient tablet, an early-model ration of algae paste a government agency had been handing out as part of a pilot program. 

It was supposed to be the food of the future—perfectly balanced, efficient, sterile. He pulled out the flat, gray-green wafer. It felt chalky and unnatural in his hand.
He looked at Sofia, her face full of hope for a perfect, painless world. He then looked at his grandmother, the living embodiment of a beautifully imperfect one. With a dry snap, he broke the tablet in two. He held out one half to his sister.

“Here,” he said softly. Sofia took it without looking away from the screen, her small fingers closing around the brittle wafer. It was a simple act of sharing, a gesture Vovó had taught him. But performing it with this substance, this joyless food of the future, felt like a parody. 

It was an act of human connection using the very tool of the impersonal world to come. He would remember that snap, that sharing of the gray paste, for the rest of his life. It was the moment he cemented a bond with his sister using a symbol of the very force that would one day take her from him.

The news report ended. The hologram of Erik Vinter dissolved, leaving only the quiet gloom of the apartment. Diego walked to the window and looked out.
The rain had stopped. Below, the vast, sprawling organism of São Paulo breathed. It was a chaotic, broken, corrupt, and vibrant system. 

A city of millions of messy, unpredictable human variables all colliding and cooperating, cheating and caring, failing and surviving. The world was celebrating Erik Vinter as a hero, a genius who had identified a bug in the human operating system and was now deploying the ultimate patch. He was building a world free from the vulnerability of trust. A perfect world.

But as Diego stared out at the beautiful, flawed expanse of his city, he made a silent vow. He would not dedicate his life to creating perfect systems that treated humanity like a flaw to be corrected. He would dedicate himself to understanding that flaw. He would study the illogical, unpredictable, messy data of the human heart. He realized, with a clarity that was both terrifying and liberating, that what Erik Vinter called a bug, his grandmother had simply called life.

The Nordic Mirage wasn’t a lie. It was a dangerously incomplete truth, an equation that solved for theft but forgot to carry the one for the human soul. And in the sudden, heavy silence of his family’s apartment, twelve-year-old Diego Ávila could already hear the soft, final click of the cage door locking shut.  The future looked bright, for those that chose to participate.
