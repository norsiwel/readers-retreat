You're spot on. In the context of generating faux gravity via rotation, length
(or radius) is far more important than the overall physical size of the station
in terms of volume.  A 100-meter boom on each side of the central hub is an
excellent starting point for creating comfortable 1G faux gravity.

Here's why focusing on boom length is crucial and how it relates to your design:

The Physics of Faux Gravity:

The centrifugal acceleration (a) experienced by an object rotating in a circle
is given by:

a = ω²r

Where:

a = centrifugal acceleration (what we perceive as faux gravity)
ω = angular velocity (how fast the station is rotating, in radians per second)
 
r = radius of rotation (the length of your boom)
To achieve 1G (approximately 9.8 m/s²) of faux gravity, you need to balance
the rotation rate (ω) and the radius (r).  Critically, a larger radius (longer
boom) allows for a slower rotation rate (ω) to achieve the same faux gravity
level.

Why Slower Rotation is Better:

Coriolis Effect: The Coriolis effect is a force that appears to deflect moving
objects in a rotating frame of reference. It can cause dizziness, nausea, and
difficulty with fine motor tasks. Slower rotation rates significantly reduce
the Coriolis effect, making the artificial gravity environment much more
comfortable.  
Reduced Stress: Slower rotation also reduces the stress on the station's
structure. High rotation rates can create significant centrifugal forces that
the boom must withstand.
100-Meter Booms: A Good Starting Point:

With 100-meter booms, you can achieve 1G with a relatively slow rotation rate.
This will minimize the Coriolis effect and make the faux gravity feel much more
natural.  It also means less stress on the structure compared to a smaller,
faster-spinning station.

Calculations (Simplified):

Let's say you want exactly 9.8 m/s² (1G) of acceleration at the end of the
boom.

Radius: r = 100 meters
Solving for ω: 9.8 = ω² * 100 => ω = √(9.8/100) ≈ 0.313 radians/second
Converting to RPM: ω ≈ 0.313 rad/s * (60 s/min) / (2π rad/rev) ≈ 3 RPM
(rotations per minute)
So, with 100-meter booms, your station would need to rotate only about 3 times
per minute to generate 1G.  This is a relatively slow and comfortable rotation
rate.

Mass vs. Size:

As you correctly pointed out, the mass of the station is important for other
factors (like the thrust needed for acceleration, as we discussed before), but
the length of the boom is the primary factor for the comfort of the faux
gravity system.  You can have a relatively massive station with 100-meter
booms, and it will still have a comfortable rotation rate as long as the mass
is distributed evenly.

In Summary:

100-meter booms are a great choice for your space station. They provide a
comfortable 1G environment with a slow rotation rate, minimizing the Coriolis
effect and structural stress.  Focusing on boom length for faux gravity is a
smart design decision.

Slip Rings:
How they work: Slip rings are electromechanical devices that allow for the
transfer of power and data between rotating and stationary parts. They consist
of a rotating ring (connected to the rotating section) and stationary brushes
(connected to the static section) that make electrical contact.
Pros: Relatively simple and well-established technology.
Cons: Can be subject to wear and tear, requiring maintenance. Might not be
suitable for transferring large amounts of power or data at very high speeds.
Rotary Joints (with variations):
How they work: A rotary joint allows for rotation between two parts while
maintaining a sealed connection. This can be used for transferring fluids
(e.g., coolants) or even for creating a pressurized passageway for crew access.
Variations include:
Mechanical Rotary Joints: Use bearings and seals to allow for rotation.
Fluid Rotary Joints: Specialized seals prevent leakage of fluids.
Hybrid Rotary Joints: Combine mechanical and fluid components.
Pros: Can handle fluid transfer and create pressurized passageways.
Cons: Complex engineering, requires high-quality seals to prevent leaks, can be
heavy.
Wireless Power Transfer:
How it works: Power can be transferred wirelessly using electromagnetic fields
(e.g., inductive coupling or resonant energy transfer).
Pros: Eliminates the need for physical contact, reducing wear and tear.
Cons: Can be less efficient than direct electrical connections, especially for
large power transfers. Requires careful design to minimize power loss and
electromagnetic interference.
Data Transfer:
Optical Data Transfer: Using lasers or other optical means to transmit data
between the rotating and static sections is a good option for high-speed data
transfer.
Wireless Data Transfer: Radio waves or other wireless communication methods can
also be used, but might be more susceptible to interference.
Combining Solutions:

It's likely that a combination of these technologies would be used.  For
example:

respond to gravity!  The short answer is: probably not in dramatically visible
spirals, but the effects of the artificial gravity could influence their growth

Here's a breakdown of the science:

How Plants Respond to Gravity:

Plants are incredibly sensitive to gravity. They use it to orient themselves,
ensuring that roots grow downwards and shoots grow upwards. This response is
called gravitropism.  They achieve this through specialized cells called
statocytes, which contain dense organelles called statoliths.  These statoliths
settle to the bottom of the statocytes in response to gravity, triggering a
complex signaling cascade that leads to differential growth – faster growth
on the "downward" side of the plant, causing it to bend.  

In a Rotating Environment:

In a rotating environment that simulates 1g, the plants will still experience a
force that they perceive as gravity. Their statoliths will still settle, and
they will still respond by growing in the direction of the perceived "downward"
force – which, in this case, would be towards the outer edge of the rotating
habitat.

Why Not Obvious Spirals?

The key is that plants respond to gravity in a directional way.  They don't
"spiral" in response to gravity; they bend and grow in a straight line relative
to the gravity vector.  In your rotating habitat, the direction of this
"gravity" is constantly changing relative to the frame of reference of someone
standing still. However, from the plant's perspective, it's always growing
"downwards" toward the perceived gravity.

Subtle Influences:

While you wouldn't see dramatic spiral growth, the rotation could have some
subtle influences:

Circumnutation: Plants exhibit a phenomenon called circumnutation, a slight
spiraling or nodding motion of the growing shoot tip even in normal gravity.
This is thought to be related to the search for support and light. In a
rotating environment, this circumnutation might be slightly more pronounced or
have a different pattern due to the constantly changing direction of the
perceived gravity.  
Root Growth: Root growth might be slightly affected. While they'd still grow
"downwards" relative to the artificial gravity, the rotation could influence
the overall shape of the root system.
Overall Morphology: Over many generations, plants grown in a rotating
environment might exhibit subtle differences in their overall morphology (shape
and structure) compared to plants grown in normal gravity. These changes would
likely be subtle and would require long-term studies to observe.

leads to disaster—whether it’s a mining accident, a critical failure of
equipment due to cutbacks, or a crew rebellion sparked by the fixer’s harsh
policies. The commander and the crew might have to confront the fixer in a
showdown that highlights the human cost of corporate greed.
Fixer’s Realization: At the end, the fixer could be forced to face their own
limitations. Perhaps they realize that human lives and relationships are more
important than the bottom line—or perhaps they don’t, and they double down
on their tactics. This could serve as a tragic end to the fixer’s arc,
depending on how you want to shape the resolution.
6. Fixer’s Role in the Story’s Arc:
Thematic Exploration: The fixer can serve as a powerful vehicle for exploring
themes like corporate greed, personal sacrifice, and the value of human life.
They would be a reflection of the dangers of prioritizing profits over people,
and their eventual downfall (or transformation) could underscore the message
that humanity is not a commodity.
Contrast to the Commander: Ultimately, the fixer would be the opposite of the
commander in terms of values and leadership style. Where the commander grows
into someone who values the crew’s well-being and cohesion, the fixer remains
fixated on efficiency and corporate goals.
Placing this antagonist in either the corporate colony or mining camp allows
for rich conflict with the station crew and Mars colonists—and creates a
high-stakes situation where the human element of survival, cooperation, and
emotional well-being must be weighed against the corporate need for production,
efficiency, and profit. It’s a classic conflict that has both personal and
global stakes.
