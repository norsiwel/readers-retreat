The Message
by Norsiwel

Prologue: A Message in a Bottle

The late 20th century, a time of both great promise and profound uncertainty on the small, blue planet Earth. The year was 1977. Humanity, having taken its first tentative steps beyond its atmosphere, turned its gaze outwards, driven by an insatiable curiosity about the cosmos. This era birthed the Voyager program, a bold endeavor to explore the outer reaches of the solar system. A time when computers filled entire rooms and the internet was in its infancy, humanity launched a message into the void.  Conceived in the fertile minds of NASA engineers, the Voyager missions were a testament to human ingenuity. Originally designed as a "Grand Tour" of the gas giants – Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune – the alignment of these planets in the late 1970s presented a once-in-176-year opportunity. Two probes, Voyager 1 and Voyager 2, were constructed, each a marvel of miniaturized technology for their time. Launched in 1977, just weeks apart, they embarked on their epic journeys. In a time before smartphones, before widespread digital communication as we know it, these probes carried a message from a world on the cusp of a technological revolution.  These were not mere robotic explorers; they were emissaries. Attached to each probe was a 12-inch gold-plated copper phonograph record, a time capsule known as the Voyager Golden Record. This record was more than just a collection of sounds and images; it was a carefully curated archive of human civilization, intended as a message to any extraterrestrial civilization that might one day encounter them. Encoded on the record were greetings in 55 languages, from ancient Sumerian to modern Mandarin, a medley of Earth's natural sounds – the whisper of wind, the crash of waves, the calls of animals – and musical selections spanning various cultures and eras, from Bach to Chuck Berry, from Azerbaijani mugham to Peruvian panpipes. There were also 116 images: photographs and diagrams of human life, landscapes, scientific diagrams, and even a diagram illustrating the location of Earth within the Milky Way galaxy, a cosmic address for any potential recipients. Carl Sagan and his team meticulously selected the contents of the record, aiming to provide a comprehensive portrait of humanity and its world.  The Voyagers fulfilled their primary mission with spectacular success, returning breathtaking images and invaluable scientific data about the outer planets and their moons. But their journey didn't end there. As they slingshotted past Neptune, they continued their outward trajectory, escaping the gravitational pull of the Sun and venturing into interstellar space.  These two probes, now billions of kilometers from their home world, became the first human-made objects to enter interstellar space, sailing through the vast cosmic ocean between the stars. Silent, solitary messengers carrying a snapshot of humanity, a message in a bottle cast into the cosmic sea. They drifted, and still drift, carrying within them not just data and images, but a question: Are we alone? And perhaps, more importantly, a plea: Do we matter?  On a world impossibly distant, under a sky unknown to human eyes, that question, that plea, might one day be answered. The golden record, a testament to human curiosity and a symbol of our place in the universe, may yet become the opening chapter in a story far grander than we could ever imagine. A story that would determine whether humanity would be judged worthy of a reply.  Borogorath, Protocol Officer for Interstellar  Relations, matched the unknown contact on his sensors, speed and vector and pulled alongside,  puzzeled that something so primitave could be found here so far out in the void between systems.    It was obviously very old and had been travelling  for a long time, even his advanced sensors would not have detected its tiny presence, if not for his alertness in advance of his upcoming  meeting with the Relk a new player in the  galactic  scene that he was  approaching with trepidation and  hopeful expectations. 
          Borogorath, a mid-level bureaucrat on a mission of interstellar representation, stumbled upon a sight that would forever alter his perception of the cosmos. It was Voyager, a relic of a bygone era, a testament to an unknown race's yearning for connection amidst the vast expanse of the universe.  To Borogorath, a being accustomed to the sleek, efficient technology of an advanced civilization, Voyager appeared as an anomaly, an artifact from a forgotten age. Its weathered exterior, scarred by years of cosmic radiation and interstellar dust, spoke of a journey both arduous and profound.  As he circled the ancient probe, his multifaceted eyes absorbing every detail, Borogorath felt a strange sense of awe. This battered piece of tech, so primitive compared to his own, had traversed the unimaginable gulfs of space, carrying with it a message of hope and a testament to the boundless curiosity of its creators.  In that moment, Borogorath saw not just a relic of the past but a beacon of possibility, a reminder that even the most disparate civilizations could find common ground in their shared pursuit of knowledge and understanding. The frivolous mission he had embarked upon suddenly gained a new significance, a spark of purpose ignited by the discovery of this ancient messenger from a distant world.   Borogorath said to himself,   "An Intriguing Relic from the Unknown"  Borogorath circled the Voyager probe, his multifaceted eyes absorbing every detail of its weathered exterior. A sense of awe mingled with a hint of trepidation as he contemplated the implications of this discovery.  In his mind, he weighed the potential benefits of establishing contact with this unknown civilization against the inherent risks of exposing his own to the unpredictable forces of the cosmos.  As a representative of a complex, galaxy-wide civilization, Borogorath understood the delicate balance of power that existed among the various interstellar factions. He knew that the introduction of a new player onto the galactic stage could upset that balance, potentially leading to conflict or even chaos.  He pondered the implications of this discovery, his mind racing with possibilities and potential dangers.  Ultimately, Borogorath knew that the decision of whether or not to initiate contact with humanity rested on his shoulders. He was the sole representative of his people in this remote corner of the galaxy, and it was up to him to determine whether this ancient and battered piece of technology represented a bridge to a new era of interstellar cooperation or a Pandora's Box that could unleash unforeseen consequences.  He noted a glint of reflection of light from a circular object enthroned on the upper surface of whatever it was, and drew closer to inspect it further, this was interesting.  His race being cautious by nature had provided him with an AI powered drone on the exterior of his vessel that could go over and closely inspect this fascinating glint, so he used the control to deploy it and waited paitently for its first communication.  In just a few moments his AI assistant had created a virtual copy of the entire object, complete with the golden disc that placed so prominatly on its surface, Borogorath considered that this must have been placed in such a prominent position by choice, so it must be important and focused his attention of the disc rather than the mundane, but interesting mechanical parts of this thing, he noted that it had no sentience of its own, no AI or advanced communacative abilities, it made him scratch his head in confusion, this race must be very naive to send out such a vessel not knowing who or what might find it, his thoughts flitted briefly to the Relk the aggressive and expansionist race he was on his way to visit with a document of accord for their consideration before his advanced species was forced to deal with them in another way.  The drone transmitted a high-resolution scan of the golden disc, its surface etched with intricate patterns. Borogorath magnified the image, his multifaceted eyes tracing the lines and curves. It wasn't writing in any language he recognized, yet there was a clear sense of design, of intention. The patterns weren't random; they formed a complex visual tapestry.  "Analysis," he commanded his ship's AI.  "The disc appears to be made of gold-plated copper," the AI responded. "The etched patterns are consistent with analog recording technology. Further analysis suggests it is a phonograph record."  A phonograph record? The concept was archaic, even by the standards of less advanced species. It was a method of storing sound by inscribing grooves onto a rotating disc. The idea that this civilization would send such a primitive artifact into the vastness of space was baffling.  "Translate," Borogorath ordered, though he held little hope.  The AI worked tirelessly, comparing the patterns to countless databases of known languages, musical notations, and symbolic systems. After a considerable pause, the AI responded, its synthesized voice tinged with a hint of surprise.  "The patterns encode audio and visual information. The audio appears to be a greeting in multiple Earth languages, along with a selection of music. The visual information consists of images and diagrams representing various aspects of human life and culture.”  Borogorath was stunned. This wasn't just a random piece of debris; it was a message, a carefully crafted attempt at communication. The naiveté he’d initially perceived now seemed more like a bold act of hope, a message in a bottle cast into the cosmic ocean.  He considered the implications. This message was clearly intended for anyone who might find it, a broadcast to the universe. It was a sign of a species reaching out, seeking contact, seeking understanding. It was also a sign of vulnerability, a declaration of their existence to potentially hostile forces.  The thought of the Relk returned to his mind. Their aggressive expansionism was a direct threat to the delicate balance of the galaxy. If they were to find this probe, this message… it could be disastrous. The Relk were not known for their diplomacy or their compassion. They would likely see this message not as an invitation, but as an opportunity, a sign of a weak and easily conquered species.  Borogorath felt a sense of urgency. He had to decide what to do. Should he retrieve the probe, shield humanity from the potential dangers of galactic contact? Or should he leave it, allowing humanity to take its chances in the vast cosmic arena? The decision weighed heavily on him. He had come seeking to avert a war with the Relk, but now he faced a far more complex dilemma, one that could determine the fate of an entire species. He turned his attention back to the image of the golden record, its intricate patterns now seeming to hold the weight of a thousand worlds. The message within wasn't just a greeting; it was a plea.  Borogorath paced the bridge of his vessel, the holographic projection of the Golden Record shimmering before him. The weight of his decision pressed heavily upon him. The potential ramifications of either action stretched out before him like branching paths in a dense forest, each leading to an uncertain future.  He considered the first path: retrieval. Taking the Voyager probe, shielding humanity from the galactic stage, was the cautious, logical choice. It would protect them from the Relk and any other potentially hostile species. It would allow them to develop at their own pace, without the pressures and dangers of interstellar politics. But it also felt like an act of paternalism, of denying them their place in the universe. Who was he to decide their fate?  The second path, leaving the probe, was fraught with peril. It was a gamble, a roll of the cosmic dice. It could lead to peaceful contact with other civilizations, to an exchange of knowledge and culture. But it could also lead to their destruction. The Relk were a very real threat, and their discovery of this probe could have devastating consequences.  He consulted his ship’s AI again. “Assess the likelihood of the Relk encountering this probe.”  The AI processed the information, calculating probabilities based on the probe’s trajectory, the Relk’s known expansion routes, and the vastness of space. “The probability is low in the immediate future, within the next few Earth decades. However, over a longer timescale, given the Relk’s current rate of expansion, the probability of encounter approaches certainty within several centuries.”  Centuries. That was a blink of an eye on a galactic scale, but an eternity for a single species. Borogorath knew that a delayed threat was still a threat.  He thought of the message itself, the greetings in multiple languages, the images of human life. It was a testament to their curiosity, their ingenuity, their desire to connect. It was a message of hope, a beacon in the darkness. Could he, in good conscience, extinguish that beacon?He recalled his mission to the Relk, the document of accord he carried. It was an attempt to prevent war, to establish a peaceful coexistence. But the Relk were driven by conquest, by the acquisition of resources and territory. Would a piece of paper truly deter them?  A new thought emerged, a third path, one that combined elements of the other two. What if he could warn humanity? What if he could provide them with the knowledge they needed to protect themselves, without directly interfering in their development?  He could leave a message of his own, a warning about the dangers of the galaxy, about the existence of the Relk. He could provide them with basic information about interstellar travel, about defensive technologies. It would be a delicate balance, providing them with enough information to survive without giving them the means to become a threat themselves.  This felt like the most responsible course of action. It would respect humanity’s autonomy while also protecting them from potential harm. It was a compromise, a difficult but necessary choice.  He turned to the holographic projection of the Golden Record one last time, his multifaceted eyes reflecting the light of the distant stars. “Prepare a transmission,” he commanded his AI. “A message for the inhabitants of Earth.” He paused, considering his words carefully. “A message of warning, and of hope.” The decision was made. The next chapter in humanity's story, for better or worse, was about to begin.
